Watchkeeping Mindset and Legal Duty

Why the engineer watch exists: safe, continuous and recorded machinery operation.
Training Visual
Core Learning
Watchkeeping is active supervisionA safe engine-room watch is not a walk-through. It is a continuous cycle of checking, comparing, trending, recording and reporting. A junior engineer must know normal values, abnormal trends and the point at which the senior engineer must be called.
Standing orders are not optionalEngine room standing orders, Chief Engineer’s orders and company SMS procedures define how the watch is to be kept. They cover log intervals, call-out conditions, UMS rounds, alarm handling, handover, restricted manoeuvring and emergency response.
The watchkeeper protects propulsionThe engineering watch supports navigational safety. Loss of propulsion, steering, electrical power, cooling water or compressed air can create immediate risk to the vessel, people and environment.

Monitoring Parameters and Trend Awareness

Temperatures, pressures, levels, amps, vibration, leakages and smell — read the engine room like a pattern.
Training Visual
Core Learning
Normal values matterA parameter is useful only when compared against normal running values. Cooling water temperature, lube oil pressure, exhaust temperature, scavenge pressure, purifier back pressure, pump current and tank levels must be understood as trends, not isolated numbers.
Trend beats snapshotA temperature slowly rising for two hours may be more important than a single value just below the alarm set point. Watchkeepers must look for direction, rate of change and associated symptoms.
Use all senses safelySmell, sound, vibration and visible leaks often appear before alarms. A burning smell, unusual bearing noise, steam leak or vibrating pump must be treated as an early warning.

Alarm Response and UMS Operation

Acknowledge, assess, act and inform — never simply silence alarms.
Training Visual
Core Learning
UMS does not mean unattended responsibilityUnattended Machinery Space operation allows the engine room to be periodically unattended only when alarms, safety systems and duty arrangements are effective. The duty engineer remains responsible for prompt response.
Alarm handling sequenceOn receiving an alarm, acknowledge it, identify the system, assess severity, go to the machinery space if required, take safe corrective action and inform the senior engineer or bridge when the situation affects propulsion, power, steering or safety.
Never normalise nuisance alarmsRepeated alarms must not be ignored. A nuisance alarm may indicate poor sensor condition, wrong set point or an actual intermittent fault. All repeated alarms must be reported and investigated.

Log Keeping and Watch Records

The log is evidence of control, not a decoration after the event.
Training Visual
Core Learning
Records must be timely and truthfulEngine-room logbooks, checklists, sounding records, alarm logs and UMS rounds must reflect actual observations. Falsified or copied readings defeat safety management and can damage incident defence.
Record what mattersRecord operating mode, machinery in use, load, temperatures, pressures, tank levels, abnormal findings, actions taken, persons informed and follow-up required. Do not hide temporary deviations.
Events need narrativeWhen an abnormal condition occurs, the log should show time, observation, action, communication and result. This creates continuity for the next watch and management visibility.

Module 5 of 6 · Engine Department · STCW A-III/1

Watch Handover and Communication

A safe handover transfers the true condition of the plant, not just the time.
Training Visual
Core Learning
Handover is a risk controlPoor handover causes repeated mistakes, missed trends and delayed response. The outgoing watchkeeper must brief the incoming watchkeeper on machinery condition, abnormal trends, ongoing permits, isolated equipment, alarms, standby machinery and expected manoeuvring.
Use closed-loop communicationCritical instructions should be repeated back. The receiver must confirm understanding. This is especially important for bridge-engine communication, change of operating mode, fuel changeover and emergency actions.
Do not take over blindlyIf the engine room condition is unclear, the incoming watchkeeper must ask questions before accepting the watch. Taking over does not remove the outgoing watchkeeper’s duty to explain abnormal conditions.

Standing Orders, Call Criteria and Practical Rounds

Know when to continue monitoring, when to intervene, and when to call help.
Training Visual
Core Learning
Call early, not lateA junior watchkeeper must call the 2nd Engineer, Chief Engineer or bridge when machinery safety, propulsion, electrical power, steering, fire risk, flooding risk or pollution risk may be affected. Calling early is professional; hiding doubt is dangerous.
Rounds must be systematicA watchkeeping round should follow a consistent route: main engine, generators, boilers, compressors, purifiers, pumps, steering gear, shafting, bilges, tanks, workshops and remote panels. Each round should look for parameters and physical condition.
UMS checks are deliberateBefore leaving an engine room unattended, verify operating machinery, alarms, fuel/lube/cooling systems, bilge condition, fire safety, compressed air, steering gear availability and no unsafe work left in progress.
