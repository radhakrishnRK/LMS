Module 1 · Stability Booklet Ch.2
Free Surface Effect and Slack Tank Management
The single biggest tanker-specific stability hazard — and the one most often underestimated
▶ READ
🌊
What is free surface effect?When a tank is partially filled, the liquid surface is free to shift laterally as the vessel rolls. This shift moves the centre of gravity of the liquid toward the low side — effectively raising the vessel's G and reducing GM. The tank does not need to be nearly empty to have a significant effect — the maximum FSE occurs at approximately 50% tank capacity.
📐
Free Surface Moment (FSM)The FSM is the product of the tank's second moment of area of its free surface (I) and the liquid density. The FSM is pre-calculated in the stability booklet for each tank at each filling level. The Free Surface Correction (FSC) is FSM ÷ vessel displacement. FSC is subtracted from solid GM to give effective GM.
⚠️
Why tankers are critically exposedTankers have very large cargo tanks — often 30m × 20m in plan. The second moment of area (I = L × B³/12) scales with the cube of tank breadth. A tank 20m wide has 8× the FSE of a 10m wide tank. A VLCC with 20 slack cargo tanks simultaneously can have a combined FSC exceeding 1.0m — sinking GM to negative.
🎯
Slack tank management strategyThe primary tactic: minimise the number of simultaneously slack tanks. Fill or empty each tank completely before moving to the next — avoid having many partially filled tanks at the same time. If slack tanks are unavoidable (during loading sequence), monitor GM continuously and do not depart for sea until all tanks are either full or empty.
Free Surface Correction
FSC = FSM / Displacement = (ρ_cargo × i) / Δ
Where i = second moment of area of free surface (L × B³/12) in m⁴ · Δ = displacement in tonnes · ρ = cargo density
Effective GM
GM_eff = GM_solid − FSC_total
Where FSC_total = sum of FSC for all slack tanks · Minimum required GM_eff per stability booklet
The pocket tank trap: Small tanks — void spaces, slop tanks, ballast tanks — may seem unimportant, but if they are slack at departure they contribute FSE. A 3m × 3m bilge holding tank with free surface still contributes FSM. Always confirm slop tanks and off-spec tanks in the GM calculation.

Module 2 · Stability Booklet Ch.3
Partial Loading Conditions — Stability Throughout Loading
Stability is not a final-condition check — it must be monitored and maintained throughout the entire loading sequence
▶ READ
📊
KG versus loading progressAs cargo is loaded, the vessel's centre of gravity (KG) changes continuously. If cargo is loaded into high tanks first, KG rises rapidly. If low tanks are loaded first, KG rises more slowly. The loading sequence must be planned so that GM remains positive and above the minimum required value throughout — not just at completion.
🔄
Critical intermediate conditionsThe most dangerous stability condition during loading is often in the middle of the sequence — when large tanks are partially filled and displacement is still relatively low. At low displacement, BM (metacentric radius) is reduced, while high FSE from multiple slack tanks may simultaneously push GZ curves to near-zero. This is the "stability trough."
⚖️
MARPOL loading conditionsThe IMO/MARPOL requirements specify minimum stability criteria that must be met at all stages of any approved loading condition. Minimum requirements: GM ≥ 0.15m (or vessel-specific value), area under GZ curve ≥ 0.055 m·rad to 30°, maximum GZ ≥ 0.20m at or before 30°.
📋
Approved loading conditionsThe stability booklet contains a finite set of approved loading conditions — partial load, full load, ballast, etc. The vessel must always operate in a condition that meets stability criteria. If the planned condition is not an approved case, it must be demonstrated to the loading computer that criteria are met before departure.
Departure is a stability gate — not an arrival one. The stability booklet states that all criteria must be met before the vessel departs port. Relying on "the GM will improve once we get to sea and roll some cargo forward" is not acceptable. The condition must be demonstrated compliant before the pilot comes aboard.

Module 3 · Classification Rules · Loading Manual
Bending Moment, Shear Force and Structural Limits
Loading the cargo incorrectly can break the ship — knowing your structural limits is as important as knowing your stability limits
▶ READ
🏗️
Bending moment (BM)The hogging or sagging force acting along the ship's length due to the distribution of weight and buoyancy. Hogging: the midship section bends upward (cargo light amidships, heavy at ends). Sagging: midship bends downward (heavy cargo amidships). Excessive BM cracks the hull plating and longitudinal structure.
✂️
Shear force (SF)The transverse force trying to "slide" one part of the ship vertically past an adjacent part. Maximum SF occurs at locations where weight distribution changes rapidly — typically at bulkhead positions between loaded and empty tanks. Excessive SF damages bulkheads and transverse structure.
⚠️
Tanker-specific loading riskTankers are particularly vulnerable to structural overloading because adjacent tanks may have very different fill levels — a completely full 8m deep centre tank next to an empty tank creates extreme local shear force. Never load adjacent tanks to maximum fill and minimum fill simultaneously without checking the loading computer.
📊
Reading the loading computer outputThe loading computer outputs BM and SF as percentages of the allowable limit at each frame or section. Values must remain below 100% throughout the voyage — typically the computer flags values above 90% as a warning. The still water BM/SF must be within limits at all times — wave-induced loads are added by the classification rules margin.
BM/SF limits are structural — not administrative. A vessel operating with BM at 105% of allowable is at immediate risk of hull failure. Unlike stability criteria (which have a time component), structural limits can be exceeded catastrophically with no recovery possible. Zero tolerance for limit exceedances in port or at sea.

Module 4 · MARPOL Annex I · SOLAS Ch. II-1
Damage Stability Awareness
Required residual stability after flooding — and why MARPOL damage cases must be understood, not just stored in a booklet
▶ READ
💥
Damage stability — what it meansDamage stability is the residual buoyancy and stability that a vessel retains after flooding one or more compartments due to grounding, collision, or structural failure. MARPOL Annex I specifies damage cases for tankers — typically one or two compartment flooding in any location. The vessel must survive these cases with minimum residual GM and GZ values.
📐
MARPOL damage requirements for tankersUnder MARPOL Annex I Regulation 23, crude oil tankers ≥20,000 DWT must demonstrate survival after one compartment flooding with: positive GM throughout, GZ ≥ 0.10m at 20° minimum, range of positive stability ≥ 20°. These are minimum criteria — the stability booklet shows the actual required values for each damage case.
⚠️
The loading condition connectionDamage stability is not calculated for a fixed condition — it is checked for every approved loading condition. A loading condition that gives excellent intact stability may fail the damage case if the vessel is at an unfavourable KG. The approved loading conditions in the booklet already have this checked — do not deviate from them.
🔢
Probabilistic vs deterministicModern tankers use a probabilistic damage stability method (Part B of SOLAS Ch.II-1) which calculates an attained subdivision index A that must exceed the required index R. The required index is a function of vessel length. The loading computer checks A ≥ R automatically — but the officer must understand that varying from approved conditions invalidates this check.
Flooding the ballast tank is not an approved damage case on many tankers. The ballast tanks on double-hull tankers are often assumed to be "non-critical." But a major collision or grounding can simultaneously flood both the ballast tank and the adjacent cargo tank — this is a two-compartment case. Always check the worst-case scenario for your actual loading condition.

Module 5 · Loading Computer Manual · Class Requirements
Loading Computer Use and Manual Backup Calculations
Using the loading computer correctly — and knowing what to do when it fails
▶ READ
📥
Input sequence — what the computer needsEvery tank entry must be verified: tank number, liquid type (cargo/ballast/bunker/fresh water), fill level or sounding/ullage, and liquid density. Entering the wrong density is the most common error — cargo density changes between voyages and must come from the bill of lading or terminal certificate. Default densities are dangerous.
📤
Output — what to verifyAlways check: (1) displacement matches expected, (2) KG and GM are reasonable, (3) BM and SF are within limits, (4) trim matches draughts on screen vs actual. If any output seems unexpected — investigate before accepting. The computer gives wrong answers when given wrong inputs.
🔄
Verification against actual draughtsCalculate expected draughts from the loading computer output and compare against actual gauge readings before departure. A difference of more than 3–5cm requires investigation — it indicates an input error, unaccounted mass (stores, crew, garbage), or incorrect density data.
📝
Manual backup — when the computer failsThe vessel must be capable of calculating stability manually from the stability booklet. Key manual process: determine displacement from draft marks → look up KM from hydrostatic tables → calculate GM from VCG (sum of moment/displacement) → subtract FSC from booklet tables. At minimum, the C/O must be able to determine if the condition is safe using manual tables.
Class requirement — type approval: The loading computer must have a current type approval certificate and must be verified annually against approved test conditions. If the test conditions are not on board or have not been run recently, the computer's output is not acceptable to Port State Control. Keep test condition printouts filed with the computer documentation.

Module 6 · SIRE 2.0 · Paris MOU PSC
Inspector Expectations — Stability Knowledge and Documentation
What SIRE and PSC inspectors ask, what they look for, and what answers demonstrate real competency
▶ READ
SIRE 2.0 — "Does the C/O understand the vessel's stability limitations and the impact of free surface?"
Inspector will ask: What is the minimum required GM for this vessel? (State vessel-specific value from stability booklet). What is the current effective GM? (Solid GM minus total FSC from all slack tanks). How many slack tanks do you have now? (Know the exact number and their FSM contributions). What is the largest single FSC contribution? (Know the worst tank by name).
PSC — "Show me the stability calculation for departure."
Must produce: Stability printout from departure condition, signed by Master. All slack tanks at departure identified with FSC applied. GM meets or exceeds minimum required value. BM and SF within limits. Draft observations confirming the calculated condition. A missing or unsigned printout is a deficiency.
PSC — "Demonstrate a manual calculation of trim."
Process: Read mean draft → enter hydrostatic tables for TPC (tonnes per centimetre) and LCF (longitudinal centre of flotation) → for any added weight: Trim change = (w × d) / (TPC × LBP) × 100 cm, where d = distance of weight from LCF. Change in draught fwd = trim change × (LCF/LBP). Must be able to execute this from stability booklet without computer.
SIRE 2.0 — "What are the MARPOL damage stability requirements for this vessel?"
Must state: One compartment flooding in any location must be survived. Residual GM ≥ positive throughout damage. GZ ≥ 0.10m at 20° minimum. Range of positive stability ≥ 20°. Be able to locate the damage stability cases in the stability booklet and point to the worst case for the current loading condition.
The SIRE inspector is not trying to catch you out — they want to know that you actually understand stability, not just operate the computer. Officers who can only read a printout but cannot explain what GM means or why FSE is dangerous are noted as a hazard observation. Know the concepts as well as the calculations.
