Module 1 · MARPOL Annex VI · ISGOTT Ch.14
Purpose of VEC — Environmental, Commercial and Regulatory
Why VEC exists, who requires it, and the commercial and environmental case for vapour recovery
▶ READ
🌍
What are VOCs and why control them?Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs) are the light hydrocarbon vapours that evaporate from petroleum cargo during loading. When released to atmosphere, VOCs contribute to photochemical smog, ground-level ozone formation, and are toxic to human health at high concentrations. MARPOL Annex VI Regulation 15 requires VOC management plans and VEC systems at terminals that require it.
⚖️
Regulatory frameworkMARPOL Annex VI Reg.15: tankers carrying crude oil must have an approved VOC Management Plan. Terminals may require VEC connection as a condition of loading — particularly in the USA (EPA 40 CFR Part 60) and increasingly in the EU and major crude oil terminals globally. A vessel without a VEC system may be refused loading at these terminals.
💰
Commercial benefit — cargo preservationEvery tonne of vapour released during loading is cargo lost. High-vapour-pressure cargoes (naphtha, light crude oil, gasoline components) can generate significant VOC losses during loading — the value recovered by vapour capture is commercially significant. On a 100,000 MT cargo at 0.3% vapour loss, 300 MT of cargo may be recovered by effective VEC.
🏭
EPA rules — USA port requirementsUS EPA 40 CFR Part 60 Subpart XX mandates vapour recovery at US marine terminals handling certain petroleum products. Tankers calling at US terminals must be equipped with a VEC system that meets specific performance standards. Non-compliance means the terminal cannot load the vessel — no VEC capability = no US terminal access for controlled cargoes.
📋
VOC Management Plan (VOCAMP)Required by MARPOL Annex VI for all crude oil tankers regardless of VEC fitting. The VOCAMP describes: the vessel's vapour generation during cargo operations, measures to minimise vapour generation, procedures for VEC system use (if fitted), and record-keeping. Must be approved by the flag state and carried on board.
🔒
VEC system type approvalLike the COW Manual, the VEC system must be type-approved and its operation must comply with the vessel's VEC Operations Manual. The Manual specifies maximum flow rates, pressure limits, approved cargoes, and emergency procedures. Operation outside Manual limits is non-compliant.
MARPOL Annex VI Regulation 15.6: The master of a tanker fitted with a VEC system must ensure that the system is in good working order and that appropriate records of its use are maintained. These records are inspected by Port State Control — a non-functioning VEC system or absent records is a deficiency.

Module 3 · VEC Operations Manual §3 · ISGOTT §14.3
Vapour Handling During Loading — Pressure Balance and Flow Rates
Tank pressure is the critical parameter during VEC operations — understanding what drives it prevents both overpressure and excessive vacuum
▶ READ
⬆️
Vapour generation during loadingAs cargo is pumped into a tank, it displaces the inert gas and air/vapour mixture above it. This generates a vapour flow from the tank into the VEC header. The rate of vapour generation is directly proportional to the loading rate — faster loading generates more vapour and higher pressure in the VEC system.
⚖️
The pressure balanceDuring VEC operation, tank pressure is determined by: vapour generated (loading rate), vapour flowing to shore (VEC flow rate), and IGS supply pressure. If vapour generation exceeds VEC extraction, pressure rises. If VEC extraction exceeds generation, pressure drops toward vacuum. Both extremes are hazardous.
🌡️
Vapour composition and temperatureVapour composition varies by cargo type. Light cargoes (naphtha, condensate) generate vapour quickly even at low temperatures. Warm cargo generates significantly more vapour than cold cargo. The shore vapour recovery unit (VRU) is designed for a specific inlet composition and flow rate — exceeding these causes VRU overload and back-pressure.
🚨
Overpressure protection sequenceIf VEC header pressure rises above the high-high alarm setpoint: (1) P/V breaker opens — vents to atmosphere as a safety relief, (2) high-high alarm activates on the bridge, (3) if pressure continues rising — VEC operations must stop and loading rate reduced. The P/V breaker is a safety device — its operation represents a VEC system failure that must be investigated.
VEC header vacuum is as dangerous as overpressure. If vapour extraction rate is too high, the header pressure drops below atmospheric. This can draw air into the vapour system through any imperfect seal — creating a flammable mixture in the VEC line. The P/V breaker vacuum relief opens to prevent this, but the condition must be investigated immediately.

Module 4 · ISGOTT §14.4 · VEC Operations Manual §4
IGS and VEC Compatibility — Sequencing and Interlocks
VEC and IGS interact in ways that can create hazardous conditions — the sequencing rules are not optional
▶ READ
🔗
Why IGS and VEC interactBoth IGS and VEC are connected to the cargo tank vapour space. IGS delivers inert gas to maintain positive pressure and O₂ below 8%. VEC extracts vapour from the same vapour space to send to shore. Running both simultaneously requires careful management — the IGS must supply gas faster than VEC extracts it, or the tank will go to vacuum.
🔄
The sequencing requirementDuring loading with VEC: cargo displaces vapour up into the VEC header. IGS is NOT normally running during loading with VEC — the cargo itself is displacing gas, not the IGS. However, IGS must be running during discharge — and if any discharge is occurring simultaneously with loading (rare but possible), IGS and VEC must be carefully balanced.
⚠️
The critical interlock — tank O₂ monitoringVapour extracted from a cargo tank during loading may contain O₂ if the tank was not properly inerted before loading began. Shore vapour recovery units can be damaged or create a flammable mixture if they receive high-O₂ vapour. Many terminals require O₂ ≤ 2% in vapour delivered to VRU — the vessel's VEC system must have O₂ monitoring at the vapour outlet.
🛑
Pre-loading inerting requirementBefore loading any cargo on a vessel with VEC, all tanks to be loaded must be inerted (O₂ <8%) before connecting to the VEC system. Delivering high-O₂ vapour to the shore VRU can: (a) create a flammable mixture in the VRU, (b) trigger automatic VRU shutdown, (c) expose the vessel to liability for VRU damage. Confirm tank O₂ readings before opening tank vapour valves to the VEC header.
Never open a tank vapour valve to the VEC header without first confirming the tank O₂ is below 8%. If the tank is still air-filled and its vapour valve is opened to the VEC header, the air-rich vapour flows to shore where it can create a flammable mixture in the VRU — potentially destroying the shore facility and injuring terminal workers. Pre-loading inerting is a VEC safety prerequisite.

Module 5 · VEC Operations Manual §5 · ISGOTT §14.5
Vapour Hose Connection, Disconnection and Emergency Closure
The vapour hose connection is as safety-critical as the cargo hose — the procedures are not interchangeable
▶ READ
🔌
Connection sequence §14.5.1Always connect the vapour return hose BEFORE any cargo hose is connected and BEFORE loading begins. Reason: from the moment cargo starts flowing, vapour is being displaced — the return path must already be available. Connect: (1) confirm VEC system isolated, (2) connect vapour hose, (3) pressure test the connection, (4) confirm with terminal that VRU is ready, (5) open isolation valve slowly.
🔍
Leak detection §14.5.2After connecting and pressurising the vapour hose, check for vapour leaks using a calibrated portable gas detector. Check all flange connections, coupling faces, and the hose length. Any detectable vapour leak must be rectified before loading — a leaking vapour hose connection is a MARPOL Annex VI violation and an ignition risk.
🔌
Disconnection sequence §14.5.3Always disconnect the vapour return hose AFTER all cargo hoses are disconnected and AFTER loading is complete. Reason: even after loading stops, residual vapour is still present and pressure must be equalised before disconnecting. Sequence: (1) close isolation valve, (2) equalise pressure, (3) confirm zero pressure on gauge, (4) disconnect hose, (5) blank flange immediately.
🚨
Emergency closure §14.5.4Emergency closure is required when: vapour hose shows visible damage or failure, persistent vapour leak at the connection, general alarm sounds on vessel, terminal calls ESD, or VEC header pressure exceeds high-high limit. Sequence: close ship-side isolation valve immediately, notify terminal, stop loading, confirm vapour hose pressure is zero before any approach for disconnection.
Never disconnect a vapour hose under pressure. The vapour hose contains a flammable atmosphere at positive pressure. Disconnecting without first closing the isolation valve and confirming zero pressure will cause a vapour jet at the flange — an ignition risk and a direct emission to atmosphere. Zero pressure must be confirmed on the vapour manifold gauge before any disconnection.

Module 6 · MARPOL Annex VI Reg.15 · SIRE 2.0
MARPOL Annex VI Compliance and Record-Keeping
What must be recorded, what SIRE inspectors check, and the VOC Management Plan requirement
▶ READ
📓
VEC operations logEvery VEC operation must be recorded: date/port, cargo type, loading start/stop times, VEC system connection and disconnection times, vapour header pressure range throughout loading, any alarms activated and actions taken, P/V breaker operation (if any), total cargo loaded. This log is separate from the ORB and is a MARPOL Annex VI record.
📋
VOC Management Plan (VOCAMP)Required by MARPOL Annex VI Reg.15.6 for all crude oil tankers. Content: vessel-specific VOC emission estimates for loading, carriage, and ballasting operations; measures to minimise VOC generation; procedures for VEC system use (if fitted); record-keeping requirements. Must be approved by the flag state and on board at all times.
🛠️
VEC system maintenance recordsAnnual survey and testing records, monthly P/V breaker liquid level checks, pressure sensor calibration records, flow meter calibration. All maintenance records retained for minimum 3 years. Type approval certificate must be current. Test loading condition results for the VEC system must be on file.
🏛️
Port State Control — VEC deficienciesPSC inspectors check: VOCAMP on board and approved, VEC operations log complete for recent operations, VEC system operational (may require operational demonstration), vapour manifold flange blanked when not in use, P/V breaker in serviceable condition. Absent log, missing VOCAMP, or non-functional VEC system — all result in detainable deficiencies at US ports.
SIRE 2.0 — "Is the vessel's VOC Management Plan approved, on board, and complete?"
Inspector will check: VOCAMP on board with flag state approval, covers all VOC-generating operations (loading, carriage, ballasting), VEC system described accurately (or explicitly states vessel is not fitted with VEC), records of VEC operations maintained per the Plan. For US terminal calls: confirm VOCAMP is specifically referenced in the SSSCL.
SIRE 2.0 — "Demonstrate knowledge of the VEC emergency closure procedure."
Officer must state: Close ship-side isolation valve immediately, notify terminal, stop loading, confirm zero pressure on vapour manifold gauge before any hose approach, record P/V breaker operation if it occurred, investigate cause before restarting VEC. Inspectors often ask: "At what pressure does your P/V breaker open?" — Know your vessel-specific values from the VEC Operations Manual.
