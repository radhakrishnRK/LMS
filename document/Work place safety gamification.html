Learning Content
Hazards are potential sources of harm. They can be physical (sharp objects, heights), chemical (toxic substances), biological (pathogens), or ergonomic (repetitive strain).
Effective hazard identification requires systematic observation, incident history review, and employee feedback. Always document findings.
Common workplace hazards include slips and falls, electrical hazards, noise exposure, poor lighting, and inadequate ventilation.
Key Point: Early identification prevents incidents before they occur. Make hazard spotting a daily habit.

Learning Content
Risk assessment combines the probability of an event occurring with the severity of its consequences. Risk = Likelihood × Severity.
Use a risk matrix to categorize risks: Low (Green), Medium (Yellow), High (Red). This helps prioritize control measures.
Reassess risks regularly, especially after incidents, near-misses, or process changes.
Best Practice: Involve employees in risk assessment—they often identify hazards that management misses.

Learning Content
Hierarchy of Controls: Elimination → Substitution → Engineering Controls → Administrative Controls → PPE.
Elimination is most effective (remove the hazard entirely). PPE is the least effective (last resort only).
Ensure all controls are documented, communicated, and regularly reviewed for effectiveness.
Remember: Never rely on PPE alone. Always implement higher-level controls first.

Learning Content
Every workplace must have an emergency response plan. Know evacuation routes, assembly points, and emergency contacts.
First aid, fire extinguisher use, and CPR training are essential. Practice drills regularly to ensure readiness.
In emergencies: Alert others → Evacuate safely → Account for personnel → Report to authorities → Document incident.
Critical: Never attempt heroics. Your safety and that of others is the priority.
