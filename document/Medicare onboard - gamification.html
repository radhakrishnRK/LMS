Module 1 of 6 · STCW A-VI/4-2 · IMGS Ch.1
Patient Assessment and Clinical Priorities
Systematic assessment is the foundation of every medical decision at sea
▶ READ
🅰️
A — AirwayIs the airway open and maintained? Look for obstruction, stridor, or gurgling. Apply jaw thrust if unconscious — avoid head tilt if spinal injury suspected. Consider airway adjuncts.
🅱️
B — BreathingRate, depth, effort, symmetry of chest movement. Measure SpO₂. Listen for abnormal sounds. Normal adult rate: 12–20/min. Below 8 or above 30 = critical.
🩸
C — CirculationPulse rate and quality. Blood pressure. Stop catastrophic haemorrhage. Assess skin colour, temperature and capillary refill (normal <2 seconds). Signs of shock?
🧠
D — Disability (Neurology)Level of consciousness using AVPU: Alert / Voice response / Pain response / Unresponsive. Pupils: equal, reactive to light? Limb movement symmetrical?
AVPU scale — use for every patient: A = Alert (eyes open, responds normally). V = responds to Voice. P = responds to Pain only. U = Unresponsive. Any level below A requires urgent attention and TMAS consultation.
Vital sign Normal adult range Action threshold
Respiratory rate 12–20 /min <8 or >30 → immediate action
Heart rate 60–100 bpm <40 or >150 → assess urgently
Blood pressure (systolic) 90–140 mmHg <90 → shock; >180 → hypertensive crisis
SpO₂ (oxygen saturation) 95–100% <92% → administer O₂; <85% → critical
Temperature 36.5–37.5°C <35°C → hypothermia; >38.5°C → fever
GCS (Glasgow Coma Scale) 15 (max) ≤8 → unable to protect airway; <15 → deterioration

Module 2 of 6 · STCW A-VI/4-2 · IMGS Ch.2–5
Major Trauma, Internal Injuries & Acute Conditions
Recognition, priorities, and management of life-threatening conditions at sea
▶ READ
💥
Major trauma — primary survey firstImmediately life-threatening: airway obstruction, tension pneumothorax, massive haemorrhage, open chest wound, cardiac tamponade. These kill in minutes. Find and fix before anything else.
🩸
Haemorrhagic shock — 4 classesClass I: up to 750ml loss — minimal signs. Class II: 750–1500ml — tachycardia. Class III: 1500–2000ml — hypotension + confusion. Class IV: >2000ml — life-threatening, immediate action.
🫀
Acute MI — recognitionCentral crushing chest pain, radiation to jaw/arm, sweating, nausea, dyspnoea. 12-lead ECG if available. Aspirin 300mg immediately if no contraindication. Arrange urgent TMAS consultation.
🧠
Stroke — FAST assessmentFace drooping (ask to smile). Arms — one drifts down? Speech — slurred or wrong words? Time — record exact onset time. Do not give aspirin if stroke suspected (may be haemorrhagic).
🤰
Obstetric emergenciesPre-eclampsia: hypertension (>140/90) + headache + visual disturbance. Eclampsia: convulsions in pregnancy. Postpartum haemorrhage: >500ml blood loss after delivery. All require immediate TMAS.
🦷
Dental emergenciesDry socket: painful empty socket 3–5 days post-extraction — pack with zinc oxide eugenol. Periapical abscess: severe localised pain, swelling, fever. Antibiotics per IMGS. Refer to TMAS.
When in doubt — call TMAS. The Telemedical Assistance Service exists precisely for situations where you are unsure. Early consultation is always better than delayed consultation. It is never wrong to call.

Module 3 of 6 · STCW A-VI/4-2 · IMGS Appendix
Medications, Anaesthesia and Procedures In Extremis
Using the ship's medical chest correctly — always in consultation with TMAS
▶ READ
Fundamental principle: All medications administered at sea should be done with TMAS consultation wherever possible. The medications below are those an officer may need to administer before or during a TMAS call in a life-threatening situation. Always refer to the IMGS and ship's formulary for current dosages.
💊
Aspirin 300mg (Antiplatelet)
Route: Oral (chewed, not swallowed whole) · Indication: Suspected acute MI · Contraindications: Active bleeding, known allergy, age <16, anticoagulant therapy
⚠ Do NOT give if stroke suspected — may worsen haemorrhagic stroke
💉
Morphine / Opioid analgesia
Route: IM or IV (IV only with TMAS guidance) · Indication: Severe pain — fractures, trauma, MI · Titrate to effect · Start with low dose
⚠ Respiratory depression risk — have naloxone available. Never in head injury unless airway managed.
🩹
Adrenaline (Epinephrine) 1:1000
Route: IM (anterolateral thigh) · Indication: Anaphylaxis — 0.5mg IM adult dose · Can repeat after 5 minutes if no response
⚠ For anaphylaxis — this is a life-saving drug. Do not delay while waiting for TMAS if airway is compromised.
🦠
Antibiotics (Amoxicillin / Co-amoxiclav / Ciprofloxacin)
Route: Oral or IM/IV · Indication: Bacterial infections per IMGS tables · Choice depends on suspected source — refer to IMGS antibiotic guide
⚠ Always check for penicillin allergy before amoxicillin. Consider resistance patterns.
🫀
GTN (Glyceryl trinitrate) spray
Route: Sublingual spray · Indication: Chest pain consistent with cardiac origin · 1–2 sprays — do not use if BP <90 systolic
⚠ Contraindicated with recent phosphodiesterase inhibitor use (sildenafil etc)
Anaesthesia in extremis: Surgical procedures at sea — such as wound closure, drainage, or emergency procedures — may require local anaesthesia or sedation. Ketamine is the agent most commonly referenced in the IMGS for procedural sedation. Never attempt without TMAS guidance and documented consent where possible.

Module 4 of 6 · STCW A-VI/4-2 · IMGS Ch.1 §1.3
Telemedicine and Radio Medical Consultation
Effective TMAS communication can be the difference between correct and incorrect treatment at sea
▶ READ
📡
What is TMAS?Telemedical Assistance Service. Free to all SOLAS vessels. Available 24/7. Staffed by physicians with maritime emergency experience. Contact via Inmarsat, HF radio, or satellite phone. Your flag state designates the TMAS for your vessel.
📋
What to prepare before callingPatient name, age, nationality. History and onset. Vital signs (all). Medications given. Ship's position, speed, ETA nearest port. Weather and sea state for evacuation assessment. What you need from them.
🚁
MEDEVAC decision criteriaTMAS recommends. Master decides. Factors: nature of illness, deterioration trend, distance from port, helicopter range (~200nm for SAR), sea state, availability of resources. Document the decision and reasoning.
📝
During and after the callOne person speaks — another records. Read back all instructions. Log the TMAS reference number. Record all advice received. Follow up calls as agreed — typically every 4–6 hours for serious cases. Keep a written log.
TMAS contact information should be posted in the medical room and on the bridge. For Indian-flagged vessels: MRCC Mumbai (+91-22-2150-4444). For UK-flagged: CIRM Rome or local MRCC. Check your ship's medical emergency card for the correct number for your flag state.

Module 5 of 6 · STCW A-VI/4-2 · IMGS Ch.7 · IHR 2005
Infectious Disease Management and Outbreak Control
Ships are enclosed environments — a single case can become an outbreak within hours
▶ READ
🔒
Isolation and containmentSuspect case: confine to cabin with dedicated toilet. Stop sharing food utensils and surfaces. Designate a single carer. PPE: gloves and mask minimum. Notify Master and document all contacts.
🧼
Outbreak thresholdTwo or more cases with similar symptoms within 72 hours = outbreak. Initiate contact tracing immediately. Common causes at sea: norovirus (GI), respiratory viruses, food poisoning, legionella (AC systems).
🌍
International Health Regulations (IHR 2005)Certain diseases must be reported to the port health authority before arrival: cholera, plague, yellow fever, SARS, Ebola, and any unknown illness causing severe disease or death. Maritime Declaration of Health mandatory.
📢
Port health authority notificationMaster must notify port health authority in advance if there is illness on board: as soon as reasonably practicable and no later than on approach. Failure to notify is an offence under IHR 2005.
Never attempt to conceal illness from port health authorities. Under IHR 2005, ships must submit a Maritime Declaration of Health. Concealing illness exposes the Master to criminal liability and endangers port workers and the public.

Module 6 of 6 · STCW A-VI/4-2 · MLC 2006 · IHR 2005
Medical Documentation, Reporting and SMS Integration
Records protect the patient, protect the officer, and meet legal obligations
▶ READ
📓
Medical log bookEvery medical event must be recorded: date, time, patient name and rank, presenting complaint, assessment findings, treatment given, medications and doses, TMAS consultation reference. Write contemporaneously — not from memory later.
🏥
Medical repatriationMLC 2006 requires the shipowner to repatriate a sick or injured seafarer at no cost to the seafarer. Documentation required: medical report (in sealed envelope for receiving doctor), treatment record, next of kin notification.
💊
Controlled drugs registerAll controlled drugs must be recorded in a separate controlled drugs register: quantity received, quantity used per patient, quantity remaining. Two signatories. Discrepancies must be reported. Register subject to flag state inspection.
⚖️
Legal obligationsDeath at sea: inform flag state, flag state may require MCA/coast guard investigation. Notify port health on arrival. Do not disturb the body until instructed. Log all events with times and actions taken.
IMGS reference: The International Medical Guide for Ships (3rd edition) is the authoritative reference for all aspects of maritime medical care. It should be on every SOLAS vessel and every designated medical officer should be familiar with its structure.
