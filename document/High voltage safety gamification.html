Module 1 · IEC 60364 · Ship HV Safety Code
HV Hazards — Why High Voltage is Categorically Different
The physics of HV injury are not the same as low voltage — understanding the mechanism is the foundation of every safe HV decision
⚡
High voltage is defined as any voltage above 1,000V AC or 1,500V DC (IEC 60364) Marine HV systems typically operate at 3.3kV, 6.6kV, or 11kV. At these voltages, contact does not require physical touch — electricity can arc across air gaps of several centimetres. A competent person who would survive a 240V shock has essentially zero survival probability at 6.6kV without correct PPE.
▶ READ
⚡
Arc flash — the primary HV killerAn arc flash is an explosive discharge of electrical energy through air between two conductors or a conductor and earth. At HV, the arc plasma temperature reaches 20,000°C — four times the surface of the sun. The explosive pressure wave, blast, and radiant heat energy are delivered in milliseconds. Arc flash kills at working distances where the victim believes they are not touching anything live.
🌡️
Incident energy — cal/cm²Arc flash energy is measured in calories per square centimetre (cal/cm²) at the worker's face and body. At 1.2 cal/cm² — the onset of a second-degree burn. At 8 cal/cm² — fatal burns without appropriate PPE. A typical 6.6kV arc flash event delivers 40–100 cal/cm² at normal working distance. This is why HV PPE arc flash ratings matter — it is not generic safety equipment.
🦶
Step potentialWhen a conductor falls to earth (earth fault), current flows through the ground in all directions from the fault point. Anyone standing near the fault point will have different voltages at each foot — the voltage difference drives current through the body. The danger zone for step potential extends many metres from the fault point. The escape method: shuffle with feet together, never run or take large steps.
✋
Touch potentialWhen an earthed metal structure becomes live due to an earth fault, touching it creates a body-earth path for current flow. Unlike low voltage, at HV the current is sufficient to cause cardiac arrest before muscle lock-up releases the grip. The hand-to-hand current path (through the heart) is more dangerous than hand-to-foot. Touch potential can exist on structures remote from the apparent fault.
🔌
Flashover — arc through airAt 6.6kV, arc can strike across an air gap of approximately 5mm per kV — so ~33mm at 6.6kV. At 11kV, ~55mm. This means approaching a live HV conductor without appropriate insulated tools — even without physical contact — can create a lethal arc. Flashover has killed workers who believed they were maintaining a safe distance.
🧲
Induced voltage — cables and switchingHV cables that run in parallel for long distances can have significant voltage induced on an adjacent de-energised cable. Switching HV systems creates voltage transients. Even a properly isolated cable can carry dangerous induced voltage if not earthed at both ends. This is why earthing at the point of work — not just isolation — is mandatory.
The critical difference from LV: At low voltage (240V, 440V), the main risk is electrocution through direct contact. At HV, you can be fatally injured without touching anything — through arc flash, step potential, or flashover. The 1-metre "restricted approach boundary" at 6.6kV is not a suggestion — it is the minimum distance at which flashover cannot reach you.

Module 2 · IEC 60364 · Class Rules
HV System Components — What They Are and Where They Are
Every component of the HV system has a specific hazard profile — knowing the system is the foundation of safe isolation
▶ READ
🏭
HV switchboard / switchgearThe heart of the HV distribution system. Contains circuit breakers, protection relays, metering, and busbar systems at full system voltage. The most dangerous location on the vessel for arc flash — busbar faults in a switchboard produce the highest arc energy. Must be in a dedicated, locked HV room accessible only to authorised personnel.
🔄
HV transformersStep down HV (e.g. 6.6kV) to LV (440V or 220V) for ship's service distribution. Or step up for power export. High thermal mass means they remain energetically dangerous for extended periods after de-energisation — residual magnetic fields can induce voltage. Earthing after isolation is mandatory before any approach.
🌀
HV motors — thrusters and compressorsLarge propulsion thrusters, bow thrusters on large vessels, and large compressors often run at HV (typically 3.3kV or 6.6kV). They have substantial capacitance — even after isolation, residual charge can persist for minutes. Discharge time must be confirmed before approach. Back-EMF from a slowly rotating motor can reach dangerous voltages.
🔌
HV cablesTypically single-core or three-core XLPE (cross-linked polyethylene) insulated, screened cables. The screen must be earthed at both ends. Cable capacitance stores charge — an isolated HV cable feels dead but may not be discharged. Cables running in parallel have mutual inductance — always test before touch regardless of isolation status.
⚡
HV generators (high-power vessels)On drillships, FPSOs, cruise ships, and large container vessels — main generators may operate at HV. The generator output voltage exists from the moment the rotor begins to turn. Residual magnetism means a "dead" stopped generator may produce voltage if manually rotated. Mechanical isolation (shaft brake, turning gear lock) is required in addition to electrical isolation.
🛡️
Earthing systemHV systems are typically high-resistance earthed (Petersen coil or resistance earth) — not solidly earthed like LV systems. This limits earth fault current but does not eliminate it. Resistance earthing means earth fault current is lower — but this does NOT make an earth fault safe to touch. The protection system must detect and clear earth faults even in resistance-earthed systems.
HV room access: The HV switchboard room must be locked at all times. Key held by the Chief Engineer and ETO only. No unauthorised entry under any circumstances. The room contains sufficient energy to be lethal without touching any conductor — arc flash from a busbar fault can injure anyone inside the room regardless of what they are doing.

Module 3 · HV Isolation Procedure · IEC 60079-14
HV Isolation and Earthing — Proving Dead at High Voltage
The sequence of HV isolation is not negotiable — skipping or reordering any step creates a lethal situation
▶ READ
🔑
Isolation vs proving deadIsolation (opening the circuit breaker) removes the primary energy source — but does NOT make the equipment safe to work on. Proving dead means confirming, with calibrated test equipment, that the voltage is actually zero. An isolated circuit breaker may have failed to open. A cable may have induced voltage. Equipment may have multiple feeds. Proving dead is mandatory — not optional at HV.
🌍
Why earthing at the point of work is non-negotiableEven a proven-dead HV circuit can become live again through: inadvertent re-energisation by another party, induced voltage from parallel cables, back-EMF from connected motors, lightning strike on the ship's structure. The earth conductor, connected at the point of work, provides a short-circuit path if the circuit becomes live — blowing protection rather than killing the worker.
📏
The restricted approach boundaryAt 6.6kV: 300mm from any live conductor is the restricted approach boundary — the minimum distance at which flashover cannot reach the worker without appropriate HV PPE. Entering within this boundary without full HV PPE (arc flash suit, insulated gloves, face shield) is prohibited regardless of any other circumstances. At 11kV: the boundary is approximately 500mm.
🔬
HV voltage test equipmentMust be rated for the system voltage (e.g. CAT IV 6.6kV or 11kV). Must have been calibrated within its calibration period. Must be tested on a known live supply before and after proving dead — to confirm the tester is functioning. A test result of "dead" from a faulty tester is more dangerous than no test at all.
The five Golden Rules of electrical safety apply at ALL voltage levels — at HV, the consequence of failing any of them is immediate fatality:

1. Disconnect from all power sources
2. Prevent re-energisation (lock out / tag out)
3. Prove dead with calibrated test equipment
4. Earth and short-circuit at the point of work
5. Provide protection against adjacent live parts

Module 4 · NFPA 70E · IEC 61482
PPE for HV Work — Arc Flash Suits, Insulated Gloves and Face Shields
HV PPE is not generic safety equipment — it is arc-flash rated equipment calibrated to the specific incident energy of the work location
▶ READ
🧥
Arc flash suit — the primary protectionA full-body garment rated in cal/cm² arc flash protection. Must match or exceed the incident energy at the working location (from the arc flash study or estimation). Made from arc-rated fabrics (e.g. Nomex, Kevlar blends). Does NOT protect against electrocution — it protects against the thermal and blast energy of an arc flash event. Must not have synthetic underlayers that melt.
🧤
Insulated gloves — electrical protectionTested and rated to the system voltage class (Class 00 to Class 4). Class 2 gloves (rated to 17kV AC) are typical for 6.6kV systems; Class 3 or 4 for 11kV or higher. Must be proof-tested before each use (inflate to check for pinholes). Latex only — no leather overgloves with cuts or holes. Must be within their test date. A glove beyond its test date is not HV PPE — it is PPE-shaped latex.
😷
Arc flash face shieldRated in cal/cm² — must match or exceed the incident energy at working location. A standard face shield is NOT arc-rated. The arc flash face shield is worn over safety glasses. Provides protection against: arc blast (pressure wave), radiant heat, and ejected particles. The face and neck must be fully covered — no gap between the shield and the arc flash hood of the suit.
👢
Insulated footwear and matInsulating boots or rubber-soled footwear rated for the system voltage. HV insulating mat placed at the work position provides step potential protection. The mat must be inspected for holes or damage before use. Standing on a damaged HV mat is worse than standing on the deck — it creates false confidence.

PPE does NOT make HV work safe — it provides a survivable last line of defence. PPE protects against the arc flash event. It does not protect against direct electrical contact. If the isolation and earthing sequence has not been completed correctly, the worker can be electrocuted before the arc flash PPE provides any protection. PPE + correct isolation = reasonable safety. PPE alone = false confidence.

Module 5 · SMS PTW System · Flag State HV Regs
HV Permit to Work — Beyond Standard LOTO
The HV PTW has requirements that go far beyond a standard LV permit — every additional requirement exists because someone died without it
▶ READ
📋
HV PTW — additional requirements vs standard LV LOTOA standard LV permit covers: identification of equipment, isolation confirmation, and authorisation. An HV PTW additionally requires: identification of ALL energy sources (electrical, mechanical, thermal), confirmation of earthing at the point of work, identification of adjacent live parts and their boundary, arc flash PPE requirement stated explicitly, HV-qualified person sign-off, and a defined emergency contact and evacuation procedure for the work location.
👤
HV-qualified person requirementFlag states and Class societies require that all HV work is authorised by and carried out under the supervision of a person holding a recognised HV qualification (e.g. Flag State HV Certificate, ETO Certificate, or equivalent). The HV PTW must name this person. An unqualified person may not issue, authorise, or work under an HV PTW — regardless of their rank or general electrical competence.
🔒
Lock-out / tag-out — HV specific requirementsAt LV, a padlock and danger tag may suffice. At HV: dedicated HV earthing clamps must be applied and the earthing confirmed (not just asserted), the isolation must be physically visible (open circuit breaker racking fully out), and the key to the isolation lock must be held by the person doing the work — not the issuing authority. Two-person rule: a second person must witness isolation and earthing.
📏
Safe approach distances on the PTWThe HV PTW must specify: the restricted approach boundary (distance at which PPE is mandatory), the prohibited approach boundary (distance at which no work is permitted even with PPE — approach by HV specialist only), and the arc flash boundary (distance at which arc flash PPE is required). These must be calculated from the arc flash study, not estimated.
The HV PTW must be suspended if any of the following occur during the work: Any unexpected energisation detected, a new earth fault alarm on the system, a fire or emergency requiring the work area to be evacuated, weather conditions that may affect personnel safety (flooding, sea spray on electrical equipment), or any doubt about the isolation status. Suspend first, investigate after.

Module 6 · STCW Medical First Aid · IEC 60479
Emergency Response to HV Incident — Do Not Touch the Casualty
The most dangerous instinct in a HV emergency is to touch the casualty — this module trains the correct instinct instead
🚨
If a person is in contact with a live HV conductor — DO NOT TOUCH THEM At HV, touching a casualty who is still in contact with a live conductor will kill the rescuer instantly. The casualty's body is live at full system voltage. Bare-hand contact from any direction creates a direct path to earth through the rescuer. No amount of urgency justifies this — two casualties cannot help anyone.
▶ READ
🛑
Step 1 — Do not approach. Isolate first.Before any approach to a HV casualty: de-energise at the highest available isolation point (main HV breaker if necessary). Confirm isolation. Only then approach — with caution, verifying the circuit is dead before touching the casualty or any surrounding metalwork. If isolation cannot be confirmed: do not approach. Call for the qualified person.
🏃
Rescue from a distance — if isolation is impossibleIf the casualty must be moved before isolation is confirmed (burning, secondary collapse risk): use a long non-conductive pole (dry wooden broom handle, fibreglass pole — not metal) to push the casualty away from the source, or pull with a dry rope looped at distance. Maintain maximum possible distance. This is a last resort — not a first response.
❤️
Cardiac arrest — the primary HV injury mechanismElectrical current through the heart causes ventricular fibrillation — the heart quivers rather than pumping. This is treatable with defibrillation. CPR maintains blood flow until a defibrillator is available. CPR must begin immediately once the casualty is safely accessible — every minute without CPR reduces survival chance by 7–10%. Sound the ship's alarm to bring the defibrillator.
🔥
Arc flash injuries — thermal burnsAn HV arc flash causes severe thermal burns — often to face, hands, and upper body. These are deep burns (potentially fourth degree) that require urgent medical evacuation. Secondary injuries include: blast-wave trauma (ruptured eardrums, internal injuries), eye damage from UV radiation, and respiratory injury from inhaled arc plasma products. Stabilise and evacuate — do not delay medical evacuation.
😵
Delayed effects of electrical injuryA person who survives an electrical injury and appears to be uninjured may still develop: delayed cardiac arrhythmia (in the hours following the incident), kidney failure from myoglobin release (muscle breakdown), neurological symptoms, and psychological effects. All electrical injury casualties must be assessed by a physician — even if they refuse and feel well. Ship's Master must arrange medical evacuation.
