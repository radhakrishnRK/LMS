Module 1: BWM Convention Purpose & Structure

The Ballast Water Management Convention (2004) was adopted by the International Maritime Organization to prevent the spread of invasive aquatic species through ballast water discharge.

Purpose of BWM Convention

Ballast water is essential for ship stability and safety during voyages. However, ballast water taken on in one region and discharged in another can introduce invasive species that damage local ecosystems. The BWM Convention establishes mandatory requirements to prevent this.

  • Prevent introduction of harmful aquatic organisms and pathogens
  • Minimize environmental, economic, and health impacts
  • Establish minimum standards for ballast water management
  • Promote development of ballast water treatment technology

Convention Structure

The Convention consists of Articles and Annexes that establish mandatory requirements for all vessels.

  • Annex I: Regulations on ballast water management
  • Annex II: Ballast Water Record Book format
  • Annex III: Certification procedures

Key Definitions

  • Ballast Water: Water taken on board to maintain ship stability
  • Sediments: Matter that settles in ballast tanks
  • D-1 Standard: Ballast water exchange (at least 95% volume replacement)
  • D-2 Standard: Ballast water treatment (specific organism concentration limits)

Module 2: D-1 & D-2 Compliance Standards

The Convention establishes two main standards for ballast water management: D-1 (exchange) and D-2 (treatment). Vessels must comply with one of these standards depending on their type and construction date.

D-1 Standard: Ballast Water Exchange

The D-1 standard requires vessels to exchange ballast water at sea, replacing at least 95% of the ballast water volume with mid-ocean water. This dilutes any organisms to acceptable levels.

  • Minimum 200 nautical miles from nearest land
  • Minimum water depth of 200 meters
  • At least 95% volume replacement
  • Applicable to existing vessels (built before 2009)

D-2 Standard: Ballast Water Treatment

The D-2 standard specifies maximum concentrations of viable organisms in ballast water after treatment. This allows for more flexible treatment methods.

  • Viable organisms >50 micrometers: ≤10 per cubic meter
  • Viable organisms 10-50 micrometers: ≤10 per milliliter
  • Toxicogenic Vibrio cholerae: ≤1 per 100 milliliters
  • Applicable to all new vessels (built 2009 or later)

Compliance Timeline

  • 2009-2013: D-1 standard for existing vessels
  • 2014-2016: Transition period with phased compliance
  • 2017 onwards: D-2 standard mandatory for all vessels

Module 3: BWM Plan & Record Book

Every vessel must have a Ship-specific Ballast Water Management Plan and maintain a Ballast Water Record Book documenting all ballast water operations.

Ballast Water Management Plan

The plan must be developed for each vessel and include:

  • Ballast water management procedures for all conditions
  • Identification of ballast water tanks and capacity
  • Procedures for ballast water exchange or treatment
  • Procedures for sediment management
  • Crew training and awareness procedures
  • Procedures for port state control inspections

Ballast Water Record Book

The Record Book must be kept onboard and contain entries for:

  • Every ballast water uptake operation (date, time, location, volume)
  • Every ballast water discharge operation (date, time, location, volume)
  • Every ballast water exchange operation (method, date, time, location)
  • Every ballast water treatment operation (system used, date, time)
  • Any non-compliance or Exceptional circumstances

Crew Awareness & Training

All crew members involved in ballast operations must be trained and aware of:

  • Importance of ballast water management
  • Procedures for ballast operations
  • Environmental and health impacts
  • Record-keeping requirements

Module 4: Treatment Systems & Operations

Modern vessels use various ballast water treatment systems to achieve D-2 compliance. These systems employ different technologies to eliminate harmful organisms.

UV (Ultraviolet) Treatment

UV light damages the DNA of organisms, preventing reproduction and causing death.

  • Non-chemical method
  • Effective for most organisms
  • Requires clear water (low turbidity)
  • No residual effect after discharge
  • Maintenance: Regular lamp replacement

Electrolysis Treatment

Electrical current creates oxidizing compounds that kill organisms.

  • Produces hypochlorous acid and other oxidants
  • Effective in various water conditions
  • Minimal chemical additives
  • Maintenance: Electrode cleaning and replacement

Chemical Treatment

Chemical additives are used to disinfect ballast water.

  • Biocides: Chlorine, iodine, or other agents
  • Effective against wide range of organisms
  • Requires chemical storage and handling
  • Environmental considerations for discharge
  • Maintenance: Chemical supply management

Combined Systems

Many vessels use multiple treatment methods for enhanced effectiveness:

  • Pre-filtration to remove sediments
  • Primary treatment (UV, electrolysis, or chemical)
  • Secondary treatment for enhanced kill rates
  • Monitoring systems to verify compliance

System Operation & Monitoring

  • Regular system checks and maintenance
  • Monitoring treatment effectiveness
  • Recording system performance
  • Troubleshooting common issues

Module 5: Port State Inspection & Compliance

Port State Control authorities inspect vessels to ensure compliance with the BWM Convention. Officers must be prepared for inspections and understand Exceptional circumstances provisions.

Port State Control Inspections

PSC inspectors may examine:

  • Ship-specific Ballast Water Management Plan
  • Ballast Water Record Book entries
  • Treatment system certificates and records
  • Crew competency and training records
  • Ballast tank condition and sediment levels
  • System maintenance and spare parts availability

Common Inspection Findings

  • Incomplete or inaccurate Record Book entries
  • Non-functional treatment systems
  • Lack of crew training documentation
  • Improper ballast tank maintenance
  • Missing or expired certificates

Exceptional Circumstances

When D-1 exchange or D-2 treatment cannot be performed due to safety or environmental reasons:

  • Safety Reasons: Severe weather, equipment failure, or navigation hazards
  • Environmental Reasons: Unsuitable water conditions for exchange
  • Documentation Required: Detailed entry in Record Book explaining circumstances
  • Alternative Measures: Retention of ballast water onboard or other approved methods

Compliance Best Practices

  • Maintain accurate and complete Record Book entries
  • Ensure all crew are properly trained
  • Perform regular system maintenance and checks
  • Keep all certificates and documentation current
  • Document any deviations or Exceptional circumstances
  • Cooperate fully with PSC inspectors

Penalties for Non-Compliance

  • Port State Control detention
  • Fines and penalties
  • Restrictions on ballast operations
  • Vessel deficiency notices
  • Potential legal action
