Module 1 · ISGOTT §11.1–11.2
Principles of Inerting and Flammability
The science that every IGS decision is based on — the flammability triangle and explosive range
▶ READ
🔺
The fire triangleThree things must be present simultaneously for fire: fuel (petroleum vapour), oxygen (in air), and an ignition source (heat/spark). Remove any one of the three and combustion cannot occur. The IGS removes the oxygen — or rather, reduces it to a level where combustion is impossible.
📊
Flammable (explosive) rangePetroleum vapour will only ignite when its concentration is between the Lower Explosive Limit (LEL) and Upper Explosive Limit (UEL). Crude oil: LEL ≈ 1%, UEL ≈ 10% by volume in air. Outside this range — no ignition is possible.
💨
How inerting worksWhen O₂ in the tank atmosphere falls below approximately 8% by volume, the flammable range ceases to exist — no mixture of petroleum vapour and the remaining atmosphere will ignite, regardless of vapour concentration. This is the inerted condition.
🎯
The 8% O₂ targetISGOTT requires tank O₂ to be maintained below 8%. At this level, even if ignition is somehow attempted, combustion cannot be sustained. The IGS continuously replaces air that enters tanks (through pumping, thermal change, leakage) with inert gas to hold O₂ below this limit.
Critical principle: A tank transitioning between inerted and gas-free must pass through the flammable range. Always purge with inert gas until HC is below LEL before introducing fresh air. Never skip the purge step.

Module 2 · ISGOTT §11.3
IGS System Components
Know every component — its function, its limits, and what fails when it fails
▶ READ
ComponentFunctionKey limit / alarm
Flue gas source / IGG §11.3.1Generates inert gas from boiler flue gas or dedicated IGG. Supply O₂ ≤5% (target <2%).High O₂ alarm: supply O₂ >5%. Auto-shutdown and divert to atmosphere.
Scrubber §11.3.2Cools gas (from ~350°C to ~45°C), removes SOx, soot, and acid gases. Water spray counter-current to gas flow.High gas temperature alarm (>65°C). Low scrubber water flow alarm. Soot accumulation causes corrosion.
Blower §11.3.3Draws scrubbed IG from scrubber and pushes it to the main deck IG distribution line. Two blowers — one duty, one standby.High discharge pressure alarm. Blower failure triggers auto-standby start.
Deck water seal §11.3.4Non-return safety device. Prevents backflow of cargo vapour into IGS plant if tank pressure exceeds IG supply pressure. Water filled at all times.Low water level alarm. If water seal fails — cargo vapour can reach IG plant.
Non-return valve (NRV) §11.3.5Mechanical non-return backup to deck water seal. Closes automatically on reverse pressure. Final safety barrier before IG plant.Periodic testing required. Failure is silent — discovered only on reversal test.
P/V breaker (high velocity) §11.3.6Safety relief — vents IG if IG main pressure exceeds safe limits (excess pressure). Prevents tank overpressure from IGS runaway. Directs vapour upward at high velocity to disperse above working level.Inspect liquid seal level. Liquid can freeze in cold weather — fill with antifreeze in cold climates.
Isolating valve (deck) §11.3.7Individual tank isolation. Each cargo tank has a dedicated shut-off valve on the IG distribution line to allow individual tank operations.Must be confirmed open before IG supply to that tank. Locked closed = tank cannot receive IG.
O₂ analyser §11.3.8Continuous measurement of O₂ content in supplied IG. Located at scrubber outlet before deck water seal. Must be calibrated regularly.High O₂ alarm at 5%. Zero and span calibration required every 24 hours during operations.

Module 3 · ISGOTT §11.4–11.6
Operating Sequences — Inerting, Purging, Gas-Freeing
The correct sequence is not optional — getting it wrong creates the conditions for an explosion
▶ READ
⬇️
Inerting a tank §11.4Starting state: gas-free (clean air). Goal: inerted (O₂ <8%). Method: fill tank with IG while opening outlet vent at top of tank to allow air to escape as IG fills from below. Monitor O₂ at outlet — stop when consistently reading <8% at all sample points. Do NOT start discharge before this is confirmed.
🔄
Purging an inerted tank §11.5Starting state: inerted (HC >UEL, O₂ <8%). Goal: purged (HC <LEL throughout, O₂ still <8%). Method: continue IG flow, venting HC-rich atmosphere from the bottom (HC is heavier than IG). Monitor HC content at sample points throughout tank — confirm <1% LEL at all locations before any gas-freeing begins.
💨
Gas-freeing a purged tank §11.6Starting state: purged (HC <LEL, O₂ <8%). Goal: gas-free (O₂ >20.8%, HC <1% LEL throughout). Method: ventilate with fresh air through portable fans or fixed ventilation. Monitor continuously. Do NOT allow entry until O₂ >20.8% AND HC <1% LEL confirmed at all levels throughout the entire tank.
🚫
The sequence CANNOT be reversed §11.6.2Never introduce air directly into an inerted tank containing cargo vapour. As O₂ rises from 0% toward 21%, the atmosphere MUST pass through the flammable range. Any ignition source at that moment causes an explosion. Always purge first — below LEL — before gas-freeing.
The most common IGS incident cause: Bypassing the purging step and ventilating directly from inerted to gas-free. The atmosphere passes through the flammable range. One static spark, one piece of equipment, one stray current — explosion. The three-step sequence (inert → purge → gas-free) exists because this has happened.

Fault Conditions — Recognition and Response
Four faults that require immediate action — and the wrong response to each that has caused real incidents

Fault conditionIndicationImmediate actionDo NOT
High O₂ in supplied IG §11.8.1O₂ analyser alarm (>5%). IG becoming "lean" — approaching flammable range if tank HC present.Shut down IG supply valve to tanks. Divert blower discharge to atmosphere. Do not resume until source fault found and O₂ confirmed <2%.Continue supplying high-O₂ IG to tanks. The atmosphere inside tanks approaches the flammable range as O₂ rises.
Wet gas / moisture carryover §11.8.2Water condensation on IG lines and manifolds. Steam visible from vents. Scrubber overflow possible.Check scrubber water flow — excess flow causes moisture carryover. Reduce water spray. Check scrubber drain. Water in tank atmosphere accelerates corrosion of tank structure.Continue to supply wet gas without investigation. Excess moisture corrodes tanks and ruins coating.

IGS plant total failure §11.8.4Loss of all IGS supply. Pressure falling on all tanks.Stop all cargo operations immediately. Close all tank valves. Notify Master and Chief Officer. Do not resume until plant repaired or shore IG supply connected. If at sea: no discharge until IGS restored.Continue discharge without IGS. Each tonne pumped out is replaced by air — the tank atmosphere moves toward the flammable range.

Module 6 · ISGOTT §11.9 · SIRE 2.0
IGS Records, SIRE Inspection, and Nitrogen Blanketing
What must be documented, what SIRE inspectors check, and how chemical tankers use nitrogen blanketing
▶ READ
📓
IGS log book requirements §11.9All IGS operations must be recorded: date/time, operation (inerting/purging/gas-freeing), tank(s) involved, initial and final O₂ readings, deck pressure readings, alarm activations and actions taken, analyser calibration. Log must be maintained continuously during cargo operations.
🔬
O₂ analyser calibration recordsZero calibration (N₂) and span calibration (known O₂ mixture) must be recorded every 24 hours during operations. Record: time, calibration gas used, zero reading, span reading, officer performing calibration. Failed calibration must be recorded with action taken.
🔵
Nitrogen blanketing (chemical tankers)Chemical tankers use pure nitrogen (N₂ from generators or cylinders) instead of flue gas IG. Nitrogen is used because: it is chemically inert and will not react with cargo, it is dry (no moisture carryover risk), and it meets cargo quality requirements for reactive or moisture-sensitive products.
⚗️
Nitrogen vs flue gas IGKey differences: N₂ purity typically >99%, compared to flue gas which contains CO, SOx, and particulates. N₂ pressure tanks have specific pressure-rated systems. N₂ use is typically continuous pad pressure — not total flood like flue gas IGS. Separate procedure manual required.
SIRE 2.0 — Typical IGS question (Question 6.1a): "Is the inert gas system operational and has it been properly maintained and documented?"
Inspector will check: IGS log completeness (every operation recorded), O₂ analyser calibration records (within 24 hours), current O₂ readings for all cargo tanks (must be <8%), deck pressure (must be positive), P/V breaker liquid level (must be at correct mark), and deck water seal level. Have all of these ready before the inspector boards.
SIRE 2.0 — Question 6.1b: "Does the officer responsible for cargo operations understand the IGS operating limits and fault response procedures?"
Inspector will ask: What is the maximum O₂ content you will accept in supplied IG? (5% — alarm threshold). What do you do if the O₂ alarm sounds during discharge? (Stop supply, stop discharge, close tank valves, investigate). What O₂ level must tanks be maintained below? (8%). What is the sequence to take a tank to gas-free? (Inert → Purge → Gas-free — never skip purging).
