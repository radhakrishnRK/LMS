Module 1: Understanding Non-Conformity

A Non-Conformity (NC) is defined as a departure from a specified requirement. In the maritime context, this means any deviation from the ISM Code, company SMS procedures, classification rules, flag state requirements, or international conventions.

Definition of Non-Conformity

According to ISM Code Section 9, a non-conformity is "an observed situation where objective evidence indicates the non-fulfilment of a specified requirement." This is not about blame — it is about identifying where the system has failed to deliver the expected standard.

Key Principle: Non-conformities are about system failures, not personal failures. They are opportunities for improvement, not punishment.

Types of Non-Conformity

TypeDefinitionExample
Major NCAn identifiable deviation that poses a serious threat to safety or environment, or a failure to implement ISM Code requirementsNo emergency drills conducted for 3 months; safety equipment expired with no action taken
Minor NCA deviation that does not pose an immediate serious threat but indicates a breakdown in the systemIncomplete maintenance records; single missed checklist entry
ObservationA finding that is not a non-conformity but could become one if not addressedWear noted on equipment that is still within limits; procedure that could be clearer

What Triggers a Non-Conformity?

  • Internal audits (ISM Code Section 12)
  • External audits (flag state, classification society, port state control)
  • SIRE inspections (OCIMF vetting)
  • Incident investigations
  • Near-miss reports
  • Crew observations during daily operations
  • Management reviews
SIRE Inspector Focus: Inspectors will ask crew members if they know how to raise a non-conformity and whether they have ever done so. A crew that never raises NCs may indicate a poor safety culture.

Module 2: How to Raise a Non-Conformity

Raising a non-conformity correctly is a critical skill for all officers. A well-written NC report enables effective investigation and corrective action. A poorly written one wastes time and may not lead to meaningful improvement.

The NC Report Structure

Every NC report should contain the following elements:

  1. Description of the finding: What was observed? Be specific and factual.
  2. Reference to requirement: Which procedure, regulation, or standard was not met?
  3. Objective evidence: What proof supports the finding? (photos, records, observations)
  4. Classification: Major NC, Minor NC, or Observation
  5. Date and location: When and where was it identified?
  6. Raised by: Who identified the non-conformity?
Writing Tip: Describe WHAT you found, not WHY you think it happened. Root cause analysis comes later. Keep the description factual and objective.

Good vs Poor NC Descriptions

Poor DescriptionGood Description
"Safety equipment not maintained properly""Fire extinguisher FE-12 in engine room workshop found with expired inspection tag (last inspected 14 months ago). SMS Procedure MP-04 requires monthly inspection."
"Crew not following procedures""On 15 March, AB was observed grinding without face shield. Risk assessment RA-2024-031 specifies face shield as mandatory PPE for grinding operations."
"Records incomplete""Engine room log book entries for 10-12 March missing main engine bearing temperature readings. Standing Order SO-ER-01 requires hourly recording."

Who Can Raise a Non-Conformity?

Any crew member can and should raise non-conformities. The ISM Code encourages reporting at all levels. However, the process for raising an NC typically involves:

  • Internal NC: Any crew member identifies and reports to the Master or Head of Department
  • External NC: Raised by auditors, inspectors, or port state control officers
  • Company NC: Raised by shore-based superintendents or DPA during vessel visits
Common Mistake: Many officers confuse a non-conformity with a deficiency. A deficiency is a physical problem (broken equipment). A non-conformity is a system failure (the maintenance system failed to identify and repair the broken equipment).

Module 3: Root Cause Analysis

Root cause analysis (RCA) is the process of identifying the fundamental reason why a non-conformity occurred. Without proper RCA, corrective actions will only address symptoms, and the problem will recur.

Why Root Cause Analysis Matters

The purpose of RCA is to determine WHY the non-conformity happened, not just WHAT happened. By addressing the root cause, we prevent recurrence rather than just fixing the immediate problem.

Key Principle: If your corrective action only fixes the symptom, the non-conformity will recur. True corrective action addresses the root cause.

The 5-Why Technique

The 5-Why technique involves asking "Why?" repeatedly until you reach the fundamental cause. Example:

  1. Problem: Fire extinguisher found expired → Why?
  2. Monthly inspection was not carried out → Why?
  3. The responsible person was not aware of the schedule → Why?
  4. The PMS task was not assigned after crew change → Why?
  5. The handover procedure does not include PMS task reassignment → Root Cause!

Fishbone (Ishikawa) Diagram

The fishbone diagram organises potential causes into categories:

CategoryMaritime Examples
PeopleTraining gaps, fatigue, language barriers, competence
ProceduresMissing steps, unclear instructions, outdated procedures
EquipmentMalfunction, inadequate maintenance, design limitations
EnvironmentWeather, workspace conditions, time pressure
ManagementResource allocation, supervision, communication
MaterialsQuality of supplies, availability of spares

Common RCA Mistakes

  • Stopping at the first "why" — not digging deep enough
  • Blaming individuals rather than identifying system failures
  • Accepting "human error" as a root cause (ask WHY the human error occurred)
  • Not involving the people closest to the problem
  • Rushing the analysis to close the NC quickly
Important: "Human error" is NEVER an acceptable root cause. Always ask: What in the system allowed or encouraged the human error to occur?

Module 4: Corrective and Preventive Action

Once the root cause is identified, the next step is to implement corrective action that addresses the cause and prevents recurrence. Understanding the difference between corrective and preventive action is essential.

Corrective Action vs Preventive Action

TypeDefinitionExample
Immediate ActionAction taken to address the immediate problem (containment)Replace the expired fire extinguisher immediately
Corrective ActionAction taken to eliminate the root cause of the detected non-conformityUpdate handover procedure to include PMS task reassignment checklist
Preventive ActionAction taken to eliminate the cause of a potential non-conformity (before it occurs)Implement automated PMS alerts for all critical safety equipment inspections
Key Distinction: Corrective action fixes what went wrong. Preventive action stops similar problems from happening elsewhere in the system.

Characteristics of Effective Corrective Action

  • Addresses the root cause — not just the symptom
  • Measurable — you can verify it has been implemented
  • Time-bound — has a clear deadline for completion
  • Assigned — a specific person is responsible
  • Proportionate — appropriate to the severity of the NC
  • Sustainable — will remain effective over time

The Corrective Action Process

  1. Take immediate containment action (if safety risk exists)
  2. Conduct root cause analysis
  3. Develop corrective action plan
  4. Assign responsibility and deadline
  5. Implement the corrective action
  6. Verify effectiveness (has the problem been eliminated?)
  7. Close the NC with evidence of completion

Verification of Effectiveness

An NC should not be closed until the corrective action has been verified as effective. This means checking — after a reasonable period — that the problem has not recurred. Methods include:

  • Follow-up audit of the specific area
  • Review of records over subsequent months
  • Crew interviews to confirm understanding
  • Master's review confirmation
Common Failure: Closing NCs with "crew briefed" or "procedure reviewed" as the only corrective action. These are awareness actions, not system changes. SIRE inspectors specifically look for evidence of meaningful corrective action.

Module 5: NC Database, Trends & Continuous Improvement

The NC system is not just about fixing individual problems — it is a powerful tool for continuous improvement when data is collected, analysed, and acted upon systematically.

NC Database Management

Every non-conformity should be recorded in a database (electronic or paper-based) that captures:

  • NC number and date raised
  • Description and classification (Major/Minor/Observation)
  • Root cause analysis findings
  • Corrective action taken
  • Responsible person and deadline
  • Verification of effectiveness
  • Date closed
  • Trend category (for analysis)

Trend Analysis

By categorising NCs and reviewing trends, patterns emerge that indicate systemic issues. For example:

  • Multiple NCs related to maintenance records → PMS system weakness
  • Repeated NCs after crew changes → familiarisation process weakness
  • NCs concentrated in one department → training or leadership gap
  • Same NC recurring after closure → corrective action was ineffective
TMSA Element 12: Continuous improvement requires evidence of trend analysis, effectiveness review, and management action based on NC data. This is a key differentiator between TMSA Stage 2 and Stage 3.

Master's Review of NCs

The Master has a specific responsibility under the ISM Code to review non-conformities and ensure:

  • All NCs are being addressed within agreed timeframes
  • Corrective actions are appropriate and effective
  • Trends are identified and reported to the company
  • The NC system is being used actively (not just for external audits)
  • Crew are encouraged to report without fear of blame

SIRE Inspector Expectations

SIRE inspectors reviewing NC records will look for:

  • Evidence that NCs are raised regularly (not only during audits)
  • Timely closure of NCs within agreed deadlines
  • Root cause analysis that goes beyond surface-level explanations
  • Corrective actions that address system failures, not just symptoms
  • Verification of effectiveness before closure
  • Master's review and sign-off
  • Trend analysis and management response
Red Flag for Inspectors: A vessel with zero non-conformities raised internally is a concern — it suggests the system is not being used, not that the vessel is perfect.
