Module 1: Restricted Visibility & Rule 19

What is Restricted Visibility?

COLREGS defines restricted visibility as any condition in which visibility is restricted by fog, mist, falling snow, heavy rainstorms, sandstorms, or any other similar causes. This is distinct from "vessels not in sight of one another" — Rule 19 applies specifically when vessels cannot see each other visually.

Key Distinction: Rule 19 applies to vessels "not in sight of one another" when navigating in or near an area of restricted visibility. Rules 12–18 (the steering and sailing rules for vessels in sight) do NOT apply.

When Does Rule 19 Apply?

Rule 19 applies when:

  • Vessels cannot see each other visually due to restricted visibility
  • You are navigating in or near an area of restricted visibility
  • Target vessels are detected by radar alone (not visually sighted)
Critical Point: Even if YOUR vessel is in clear visibility, if you detect another vessel by radar that may be in fog, you must consider Rule 19 requirements until visual contact is established.

Bridge Preparation for Restricted Visibility

When restricted visibility is encountered or anticipated, the OOW must immediately take the following actions:

  1. Inform the Master — Call the Master to the bridge as per standing orders
  2. Post additional lookout(s) — Dedicated visual and aural lookout
  3. Switch radar to operational mode — Both radars on, appropriate range scales
  4. Reduce speed — To a safe speed appropriate to the circumstances
  5. Sound fog signals — Commence appropriate sound signals under Rule 35
  6. Switch navigation lights on — Even during daytime
  7. Close watertight doors — As per vessel's procedures
  8. Put engines on standby — Ready for immediate manoeuvring
  9. Plot radar targets — Systematic plotting of all contacts
  10. Activate AIS — Verify AIS is transmitting correctly
SIRE Expectation: Inspectors will ask the OOW what actions they take when visibility reduces. They expect a structured, immediate response demonstrating familiarity with bridge standing orders and COLREGS requirements.

Module 2: Safe Speed & Sound Signals

Determining Safe Speed (Rule 6)

In restricted visibility, safe speed must be determined considering all the circumstances and conditions of restricted visibility. Rule 6 requires every vessel to proceed at a safe speed so that proper and effective action can be taken to avoid collision and the vessel can be stopped within a distance appropriate to the prevailing circumstances.

Factors to consider when determining safe speed in restricted visibility:

  • The state of visibility — How far can you actually see?
  • Traffic density — Including fishing vessels and other small craft
  • Vessel's stopping distance — At current speed and loading condition
  • Radar limitations — Sea clutter, rain clutter, blind sectors
  • Wind, sea, and current — Effect on manoeuvrability
  • Proximity of navigational hazards — Shallow water, TSS boundaries
  • Draught in relation to available depth — Squat effect at speed
Critical Rule: There is NO defined "safe speed" in knots. It depends entirely on circumstances. A vessel proceeding at 12 knots in open ocean fog may be at safe speed, while 6 knots in a congested TSS in fog may be excessive.

Sound Signals in Restricted Visibility (Rule 35)

Rule 35 prescribes the fog signals that must be sounded by different vessel types:

Power-driven vessel making way: One prolonged blast at intervals of not more than 2 minutes. (— )
Power-driven vessel underway but stopped: Two prolonged blasts at intervals of not more than 2 minutes, with approximately 2 seconds between them. (— —)
Vessels NUC, RAM, constrained by draught, sailing, fishing, towing/pushing: One prolonged followed by two short blasts at intervals of not more than 2 minutes. (— · ·)
Vessel being towed (last vessel): One prolonged followed by three short blasts at intervals of not more than 2 minutes. (— · · ·)
Vessel at anchor: Rapid ringing of bell for about 5 seconds at intervals of not more than 1 minute. Vessels ≥100m also sound a gong aft immediately after the bell.

Action on Hearing a Fog Signal

When you hear a fog signal apparently forward of your beam:

  • Reduce speed to the minimum at which the vessel can be kept on course
  • If necessary, take all way off the vessel
  • Navigate with extreme caution until the danger of collision is over

Module 3: Radar Target Assessment in Fog

Radar Limitations in Restricted Visibility

While radar is the primary detection tool in restricted visibility, officers must understand its limitations:

  • Sea clutter — Masks small targets near own vessel, particularly in heavy seas
  • Rain clutter — Can obscure targets within rain cells
  • Blind sectors — Caused by masts, funnels, and deck structures
  • Shadow sectors — Areas behind large targets where smaller targets may be hidden
  • Small target detection — Wooden boats, yachts, and debris may not show on radar
  • Indirect echoes — False targets caused by reflections from vessel's own structure
  • Second-trace echoes — Targets beyond maximum range appearing within display
Critical Warning: A clear radar screen does NOT mean there are no vessels in the vicinity. Small vessels, particularly those made of wood or fibreglass, may not produce a detectable radar echo.

Systematic Radar Plotting

In restricted visibility, systematic radar plotting is essential:

  1. Detection — Identify new targets as early as possible
  2. Tracking — Plot target position at regular intervals (every 3–6 minutes)
  3. Assessment — Determine CPA (Closest Point of Approach) and TCPA (Time to CPA)
  4. Decision — Determine if risk of collision exists (CPA less than acceptable limit)
  5. Action — Take avoiding action if required, in ample time
  6. Verification — Monitor effectiveness of action taken
ARPA/AIS Integration: While ARPA provides automatic tracking, the OOW must verify ARPA data against manual plots. AIS data complements radar but should never replace radar observation — not all vessels carry AIS, and AIS data may be inaccurate.

Range Scale Selection

In restricted visibility, the following range scale strategy is recommended:

  • Long range (12–24 nm) — For early detection and traffic overview
  • Medium range (6–12 nm) — For systematic plotting and CPA assessment
  • Short range (3–6 nm) — For close-quarters situation management

Both radars should be operational, set to different range scales for comprehensive coverage.

Module 4: Action Under Rule 19

Rule 19(d) — Actions When Detecting a Vessel by Radar

When a vessel is detected by radar alone (not visually sighted), and a risk of collision is determined to exist, Rule 19(d) prescribes specific actions:

Rule 19(d)(i): An alteration of course to PORT for a vessel forward of the beam (other than for a vessel being overtaken) shall be AVOIDED.
Rule 19(d)(ii): An alteration of course towards a vessel abeam or abaft the beam shall be AVOIDED.

Practical Application of Rule 19(d)

The key principles for action under Rule 19:

  • Target forward of starboard beam → Alter course to STARBOARD (turn away)
  • Target forward of port beam → Alter course to STARBOARD (avoid port alteration)
  • Target on port beam or abaft → Do NOT alter towards it (alter starboard or reduce speed)
  • Target on starboard beam or abaft → Do NOT alter towards it (alter port or reduce speed)
  • Speed reduction → Always an option, and often the safest first action
Golden Rule: In restricted visibility, if in doubt about the correct action, REDUCE SPEED. Speed reduction is always appropriate and buys time for assessment.

Key Differences from Clear Visibility Rules

In restricted visibility (Rule 19):

  • There is NO "stand-on" or "give-way" vessel — both vessels must take action independently
  • There is NO hierarchy of vessels (sailing, fishing, NUC, RAM do not have priority)
  • Action must be taken in ample time — early and substantial
  • A succession of small alterations shall be avoided
  • The action taken must be monitored until the other vessel is finally past and clear
Common Error: Officers sometimes apply Rule 15 (crossing situation) or Rule 14 (head-on) logic in fog. These rules do NOT apply when vessels cannot see each other. Only Rule 19 applies.

Module 5: Case Studies & Lessons Learned

Case Study 1: MV Nordic — Collision in Dover Strait Fog

A bulk carrier proceeding through the Dover Strait TSS in dense fog at 14 knots detected a target on radar at 6nm. The OOW assessed CPA at 0.5nm but took no action, believing the target would pass clear. At 2nm, CPA was reassessed at 0.1nm. The OOW made a small alteration to port. Collision occurred.

Failures: Excessive speed in fog; late action; alteration to port for vessel forward of beam (Rule 19 violation); small alteration instead of substantial; Master not called early enough.

Case Study 2: Chemical Tanker — Fog Signal Ignored

A chemical tanker in the English Channel heard a fog signal forward of the beam. The OOW did not reduce speed, believing the AIS showed all targets clear. A fishing vessel without AIS was struck at the bow. The fishing vessel had been sounding the correct fog signal (one prolonged + two short).

Failures: Over-reliance on AIS; failure to reduce speed on hearing fog signal forward of beam; failure to maintain proper aural lookout; assumption that radar/AIS showed all traffic.

Case Study 3: Container Ship — Multiple Target Confusion

A container ship in a TSS encountered patchy fog. Multiple targets were detected on radar. The OOW made a series of small course alterations to avoid individual targets, creating an erratic track. A vessel approaching from astern was not detected due to focus on forward targets. Near-miss occurred.

Failures: Succession of small alterations; failure to reduce speed when situation became confused; loss of situational awareness; failure to monitor all sectors; Master not informed of deteriorating situation.

Key Lessons for Watchkeepers

  1. Speed kills in fog — Reduce speed early, not as a last resort
  2. Act early and substantially — Small late alterations are dangerous
  3. Never alter to port for a target forward of the beam
  4. Call the Master early — Before the situation becomes critical
  5. Trust your ears — Fog signals are a vital detection method
  6. AIS is not complete — Many vessels don't carry or transmit AIS
  7. When confused, STOP — Reducing to minimum speed is always correct
  8. Monitor your action — Verify the target is passing clear after your alteration
