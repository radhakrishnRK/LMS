Module 1 of 6 · Management / TMSA
UKC Basics and Minimum Requirements

Understand static UKC, dynamic UKC, company policy, port requirements and the difference between charted depth and actual available water.

Training Visual
Core Learning
Static UKCStatic under keel clearance is the water depth available below the keel before dynamic effects such as squat, heel, waves and motion are applied.
Dynamic UKCDynamic UKC considers squat, wave response, heel, trim, density, tide uncertainty, survey tolerance and safety margin.
Policy firstCompany SMS, port rules, pilotage requirements and Master’s standing orders may set minimum UKC. The calculation must never be used to justify breaching policy.

Module 2 of 6 · Management / TMSA
Tide, Draft, Density and Trim

Build the UKC calculation from chart datum, tide height, observed draft, trim, density allowance and survey confidence.

Training Visual
Core Learning
Available depthAvailable depth normally starts with charted depth plus predicted/observed tide, adjusted for dredging information, silting and survey reliability.
Draft is not fixedDraft changes with loading, consumption, density change, trim and list. Forward and aft drafts must be understood for shallow water transit.
Fresh water and densityReduced water density increases draft. Density correction may be important in rivers, estuaries and ports with brackish water.

Module 3 of 6 · Management / TMSA
Squat: What It Is and Why Speed Matters

Understand squat as the sinkage and trim change caused by ship movement through shallow or confined water.

Training Visual
Core Learning
Squat is dynamic sinkageAs the ship moves, water flow accelerates around the hull, pressure changes and the vessel may sink bodily and trim by bow or stern.
Speed effect is powerfulSquat increases rapidly with speed. A small speed increase can produce a large reduction in UKC.
Confined water increases riskChannels, canals, banks and shallow water can increase squat, bank effect and interaction with other ships.

Module 4 of 6 · Management / TMSA
Practical UKC Calculation Workflow

Use a clear calculation sequence: depth, tide, draft, squat, wave allowance, heel/list, safety margin and final go/no-go decision.

Training Visual
Core Learning
Use a structured worksheetA good UKC worksheet shows input data, assumptions, source of tide, speed used for squat, controlling depth and minimum margin.
Worst credible caseUse conservative assumptions where information is uncertain. The shallowest point and highest expected squat are often controlling.
Communicate the resultUKC calculation must be understood by the Master, OOW, pilot and relevant team members when it affects passage safety.

Module 5 of 6 · Management / TMSA
Real-Time Monitoring During Transit

Monitor echo sounder, speed, tide window, position, traffic, helm response, bank effect and pilot-Master communication.

Training Visual
Core Learning
Monitoring is continuousThe calculation is only the plan. During transit, the bridge team must monitor actual depth, speed, track, traffic, tide window and vessel behaviour.
Echo sounder limitationsEcho sounder readings must be understood: transducer location, offset, alarm setting, seabed quality and shallow-water signal quality matter.
Bridge team challengeIf actual depth, speed or track differs from the plan, the OOW must speak up clearly and early.

Go/No-Go, Pilot Coordination and Lessons Learned

Make safe passage decisions when UKC is marginal and record assumptions, deviations and lessons for future voyages.

Training Visual
Core Learning
Master retains responsibilityPilot advice is essential, but the Master remains responsible for safe navigation and must understand the UKC risk.
Go/no-go disciplineIf UKC margin is inadequate or assumptions are uncertain, safe options include delay, speed reduction, daylight transit, additional tug support or revised passage plan.
After-action learningNear misses, unexpected depth, excess squat or pilotage concerns should be recorded and fed into SMS, passage planning and future UKC procedures.
