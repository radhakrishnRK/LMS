Scene Safety and Priorities

First actions when you find an injured or ill person onboard

📖 Read this first
⚡
Make scene safe firstMoving gear, snapback zones, slippery decks, electrical risks, chemicals, confined spaces — check before you approach.
👆
Check responsivenessTap shoulders firmly and call loudly: "Can you hear me?" — look for a response before assuming unconsciousness.
🫁
Check breathingLook, listen, feel for no more than 10 seconds. Occasional gasps are NOT normal breathing — act immediately.
🚨
Raise the alarm earlyGet help coming while you work. A second pair of hands and the first aid kit may be the difference.
Never become the second casualty. If you rush in without checking, you can be injured by the same hazard. Scene safety is not optional.

CPR and Rescue Breathing

Recognise when CPR is needed and maintain effective compressions

📖 Read this first
🔴
When to start CPRUnresponsive AND not breathing normally. Occasional gasps = start CPR immediately. Do not wait.
💪
Compression qualityCentre of chest. Hard (5–6 cm), fast (100–120/min), regular rhythm. Allow full chest recoil between compressions.
⏸️
Minimise pausesEvery second off the chest reduces blood flow to the brain. Stop only when necessary — AED shock or confirmed recovery.
😮‍💨
Rescue breathsIf trained and safe: 30 compressions then 2 breaths. If unwilling or unsure: hands-only CPR is still highly effective.
The Bee Gees rule: "Stayin' Alive" runs at exactly 100 bpm. If you know the song, you know the rate. Compress to the beat.

Bleeding Control and Shock

Control severe bleeding and recognise the signs of shock

📖 Read this first
🩺
Direct pressure firstUse a dressing, cloth, or your gloved hand. Press hard and hold — do not remove the dressing. Add more on top if it soaks through.
🤝
Reassure the casualtyFear increases heart rate and worsens bleeding. Stay calm, talk steadily, and explain what you are doing.
👁️
Watch for shockPale/clammy skin, rapid weak pulse, restlessness, weakness, nausea. Shock can develop quickly even after bleeding is controlled.
🏥
Get help comingSevere bleeding is a medical emergency. Get a senior officer and the first aid kit immediately — do not handle alone.
Tourniquet note: Only apply a tourniquet for life-threatening limb bleeding that cannot be controlled with direct pressure. Note the time applied. Never remove it once placed.

Fractures, Burns and Chemical Splash

Match the injury to the correct first response — different injuries need different actions

📖 Read this first
Remember the rule of three: Fracture = Support & Immobilise. Burn/Scald = Cool & Protect (20 minutes, cool water). Chemical splash = Flush immediately (15–20 minutes, copious water, remove contaminated clothing).
Never: Force a suspected fracture straight. Apply ice directly to a burn. Apply ointment or grease to a burn. Attempt to neutralise a chemical splash — flush with water only.

Recovery Position and Spinal Precautions

When to move, when to stay still, and how to support a spinal casualty

📖 Read this first
🚫
When to minimise movementAny fall from height, diving injury, direct blow to head/neck/spine, vehicle collision. Assume spinal injury until ruled out.
🤲
Support head and neckHold the head in the position found — do not correct any deformity. Maintain alignment of head, neck and body as a single unit.
🔄
Recovery positionUsed for unconscious casualty who IS breathing normally and has no suspected spinal injury. Keeps airway open if they vomit.
⚠️
When you MUST moveImmediate danger to life (fire, flooding) or if CPR is required. Log-roll technique with at least 2 people if movement is essential.
Never: Move a suspected spinal casualty alone. Bend, twist, or rotate the spine. Walk or sit a casualty who has fallen from height or had a serious impact.

SMS Integration and Onboard Readiness

Connecting first-aid competence with shipboard safety systems

📖 Read this first
📚
EFA links to the SMSYour first-aid competence is recorded in the ship's safety management system. Training records must be maintained and verifiable.
🗂️
Training recordsRecord completion date, trainer, and certification number. Records support PSC inspections and flag state requirements.
🧰
First aid kitsCheck regularly. Replenish used items. Check expiry dates. Ensure accessibility — not locked away during emergencies.
📄
Emergency proceduresKnow where the ship's medical emergency procedures are posted. Know who is designated as medical officer and their backup.
The three pillars of onboard readiness: Competence (trained and certified people) + Preparedness (procedures and records in place) + Equipment (kits checked and accessible). All three must be in place.
