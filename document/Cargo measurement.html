Module 1 · OCIMF Guidelines Ch.2
Closed Gauging Systems and Measurement Methods
How cargo quantity is physically measured — fixed systems, portable instruments, and their accuracy limits
▶ READ
🔒
Closed gauging — why it mattersSOLAS requires closed gauging for toxic and volatile cargoes. The cargo tank is not opened — measurement is taken through a fixed penetration or vapour-tight portable device. This prevents vapour release, eliminates the static ignition risk of open gauging, and is required for MARPOL-restricted cargoes.
📡
Fixed radar gauging (RTG)A microwave radar transmitter in the tank top measures ullage (space above cargo) continuously. Highly accurate (±1mm), not affected by vapour, no moving parts, continuous read-out. Tank calibration tables convert ullage to volume. The ship's primary gauging system.
🎵
Fixed float gaugingA float on the cargo surface transmits ullage mechanically or electronically to a deck indicator. Simpler and lower cost than radar. Accuracy typically ±5mm. Susceptible to turbulence during loading/discharge — read only in static conditions.
📟
Portable MMC / UTIMulti-Meter Combination — measures ullage, temperature, and interface (water level) through a single deck penetration fitting. Industry standard for independent measurement and witness gauging. Accuracy ±1mm ullage, ±0.1°C temperature. Always the instrument an independent cargo surveyor uses.
⚡
Electrostatic considerationsAll portable gauging equipment must be approved for use through vapour-tight fittings and must not generate electrostatic discharge. The MMC/UTI tape is coated and grounded. Gauging must not begin within 30 minutes of completing loading (static relaxation period).
📋
Independent surveyor requirementFor custody transfer (loading and discharge), both ship and terminal/surveyor measure independently. Significant discrepancy requires investigation before a Bill of Lading is issued. The ship's figures and surveyor's figures are both recorded — the BL is typically based on surveyor figures.
Ullage vs innage: Ullage is the distance from the datum point (reference gauge point) to the cargo surface — the empty space. Innage (dip) is the depth of cargo from the bottom of the tank. Most tanker systems use ullage. Tables are clearly marked for ullage or innage — using the wrong reading gives a completely wrong volume.

Module 2 · OCIMF Guidelines Ch.3
Ullage Corrections — Trim, List and Temperature
A raw ullage reading is rarely the final figure — three corrections must always be applied
▶ READ
↕️
Trim correctionWhen a vessel trims by the stern (common during loading/discharge), the cargo surface tilts relative to the tank, making the ullage at the forward end different from the aft end. The trim correction compensates for this using a trim correction table in the tank calibration tables. Applied to each tank individually.
↔️
List correctionWhen the vessel lists (heels to port or starboard), the cargo surface shifts laterally. Tanks on the low side show a lower ullage than true; tanks on the high side show higher. The list correction uses the vessel's list angle and the tank's transverse position. Applied per tank per list angle.
🌡️
Temperature correction (VCF)Petroleum expands and contracts with temperature. A Volume Correction Factor (VCF) converts the Gross Observed Volume (GOV) — volume at actual cargo temperature — to Gross Standard Volume (GSV) — volume at the reference temperature of 15°C. This is the figure used for custody transfer.
🔢
Corrected ullage sequenceTake the raw ullage → apply trim correction → apply list correction → look up corrected ullage in calibration tables to get total observed volume (TOV) → subtract free water → apply VCF to get GSV. Every step in sequence, every time. No shortcuts.
CorrectionWhat causes the errorDirection of effectSource of correction data
Trim correctionVessel not on even keel — cargo tilts fore-aftReduces apparent ullage for stern trim (adds volume). Increases for bow trim.Tank calibration tables — trim correction table per tank

Apply trim correction before looking up the calibration table. The corrected ullage (trim+list adjusted) is the value you enter into the tank calibration table — not the raw reading. Entering the raw reading and then applying a correction separately gives the wrong answer.

Module 3 · OCIMF Guidelines Ch.4
Density, API Gravity, VCF and the GOV→GSV Chain
The complete quantity calculation chain from observed ullage to metric tonnes on the Bill of Lading
▶ READ
⚗️
Density at 15°CThe fundamental property used for all quantity conversions. Measured by the terminal laboratory on a representative sample. Expressed in kg/m³ or kg/L. Crude oil: typically 820–960 kg/m³. The density is used to: (a) select the correct ASTM VCF table, and (b) convert GSV to metric tonnes.
🔢
API gravityAn alternative scale for density used in the USA. API = (141.5 / specific gravity at 60°F) − 131.5. Light crude: ~35–45° API. Heavy crude: ~15–25° API. Higher API = lighter (less dense). API gravity tables link directly to ASTM VCF tables for US-system calculations.
📊
Volume Correction Factor (VCF)Converts observed volume at actual temperature to standard volume at 15°C (or 60°F). Taken from ASTM Table 54B for crude oil or 54C for refined products. VCF depends on: (a) density at 15°C and (b) temperature of cargo at time of measurement. VCF <1 for warm cargo; VCF >1 for cold cargo.
🔗
The calculation chainTOV (total observed volume) → subtract free water → GOV (gross observed volume) → multiply by VCF → GSV (gross standard volume) → multiply by density at 15°C → metric tonnes → minus water and sediment % → net standard volume / net tonnes for BL.
Reference temperature: 15°C is the standard in most of the world. The USA uses 60°F (15.56°C). When receiving a shore figure in barrels at 60°F, confirm the reference temperature before comparing with ship's m³ at 15°C — a direct comparison without temperature alignment gives a false discrepancy.

Module 4 · OCIMF Guidelines Ch.5
Water Content, OBQ and ROB
Free water, on-board quantity before loading, and quantity remaining after discharge — the figures that protect the ship against quantity claims
▶ READ
💧
Free water measurementFree water settles at the bottom of cargo tanks and must be deducted from the total observed volume before calculating cargo quantity. Measured using water-finding paste on the UTI tape — the paste changes colour on contact with water. The interface depth reading gives free water volume from the interface table.
📦
OBQ — On-Board QuantityThe quantity remaining in the ship's cargo tanks from the previous voyage immediately before loading begins. Includes cargo residue, free water, and retained cargo. OBQ is measured and agreed with the terminal surveyor before loading. It is deducted from the loaded quantity for the Bill of Lading figure.
🪣
ROB — Remaining On BoardThe quantity that cannot be discharged and remains in the tanks after discharge is complete. Includes clingage, sediment, and cargo in piping that cannot be stripped. ROB is measured and agreed with the terminal surveyor. Terminal deducts ROB from the discharged quantity when calculating what they received.
🔄
StrippingAfter main discharge is complete, stripping pumps remove the final cargo from each tank — reducing ROB to its minimum. Effective stripping is important: unstripped cargo left on board reduces the quantity delivered to the terminal and increases apparent ship-shore quantity discrepancy.
OBQ disputes are common. If the ship's pre-loading OBQ is not properly agreed and documented before loading begins, the terminal may later claim more cargo was loaded than the ship's figures show — with the unresolved OBQ as the discrepancy. Always complete OBQ measurement and get the surveyor's countersignature before first cargo flows.

Module 5 · OCIMF Guidelines Ch.7
Ship-Shore Quantity Difference and VEF
Understanding why differences occur, what is acceptable, and how to respond to disputes
▶ READ
📊
Why differences always existShip and shore measurements are taken by different instruments at different locations at different times. Measurement uncertainty, pipe content volume, temperature differences between ship and shore, VCF table rounding, and OBQ/ROB measurement errors all contribute. A zero difference is actually a coincidence — not a sign of accuracy.
🏭
Vessel Experience Factor (VEF)A statistical ratio comparing ship's quantity to shore quantity over a minimum of 6 cargo operations. VEF = sum of ship figures ÷ sum of shore figures. A VEF close to 1.000 indicates consistent ship measurement. Used by charterers to assess vessel measurement reliability. Maintained in the vessel's records.
📏
Acceptable difference limitsOCIMF and industry practice accept a ship-shore difference of up to ±0.5% as within normal measurement uncertainty. Between 0.5% and 0.7%: investigate but typically not disputed. Above 0.7%: requires investigation and possibly a letter of protest. Above 1%: serious investigation — possible mismeasurement, MARPOL issue, or theft.
✍️
Letter of protestA formal written declaration that the ship disputes the shore measurement figures. Must be issued promptly after loading or discharge when: the difference exceeds acceptable limits, OBQ/ROB measurement is disputed, or conditions prevented accurate measurement. Preserves the ship's legal position.
Never sign the terminal's figures under protest without understanding what you are signing. A "signed under protest" endorsement on a Bill of Lading or cargo receipt does not nullify the figures — it only records disagreement. Your best protection is accurate pre-loading OBQ documentation and a correctly executed voyage measurement record.

Module 6 · OCIMF Guidelines Ch.8
Cargo Documents — BL, Ullage Reports and Protests
The documents that determine legal title, quantity, and the ship's liability — every signature matters
▶ READ
Bill of Lading (BL) ▾
The BL is the primary cargo document — it is a receipt for the cargo, a document of title (ownership), and evidence of the contract of carriage. The BL quantity is the quantity the shipowner is legally responsible for delivering. It is typically based on the shore surveyor's figures at the loading port. The Master signs the BL — this is a legal declaration of quantity received. Never sign a BL with a figure you have not verified or endorsed under protest where disputed.
Ullage Report (Ship's Measurement Record) ▾
The ship's formal record of all tank measurements — raw ullage, trim correction, list correction, corrected ullage, temperature, VCF, GOV, free water, and GSV for each tank. Prepared by the Chief Officer and signed by the attending surveyor. Must be completed contemporaneously (at time of measurement) — not reconstructed later. The ullage report is the primary evidence in any cargo dispute.
Notice of Readiness (NOR) ▾
Tendered by the Master when the vessel has arrived at the port and is ready in all respects to commence cargo operations. The NOR tender time starts the laytime clock — delay after NOR acceptance is demurrage (for which the charterer pays) or despatch (paid by the owner if loading/discharge is faster than agreed). NOR tendering time and conditions must be exactly per the charterparty — errors cost money.
Letter of Protest ▾
A formal written statement issued by the Master to place on record a disagreement with the terminal's figures, conditions, or procedures. Must be issued promptly — typically within 24 hours of the disputed event, or at the time of signing the BL if the figures are disputed. A letter of protest alone does not protect the ship unless it accurately and specifically describes the basis of the dispute.
Cargo Outturn Report ▾
Prepared at the discharge port comparing what was loaded at origin with what was measured on arrival. Includes the ship-shore quantity difference calculation, VEF, and all measurement data from both ports. The outturn report is what the receiver uses to determine if they have received the quantity they are owed. Significant differences trigger quantity claims against the shipowner or cargo insurer.
