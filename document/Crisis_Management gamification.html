Module 1 of 6 · STCW A-V/2
Human Behaviour Under Stress
People in crises do not behave rationally — understanding this makes you a better crisis leader
▶ READ
😰
PanicAn acute, contagious, irrational fear response. Spreads between people faster than information. Triggered by ambiguity, noise, and visible distress in others. Panic causes stampedes, people jumping overboard, and doors being blocked. It is preventable with clear, early, authoritative communication.
🔙
RegressionUnder extreme stress, people revert to earlier, simpler behaviours — they freeze, become childlike, follow rather than think, seek familiar faces. A crew member with 10 years' experience may become completely passive. This is not cowardice — it is a neurological response to perceived helplessness.
🎯
Adaptive responsesWhen people receive clear instructions from a credible source, understand what is happening, and believe they can influence the outcome, they act effectively. Adaptive behaviour includes helping others, following procedures, reporting information, and taking purposeful action.
⏱️
Time compressionUnder stress, people perceive time very differently. Events feel both faster and slower simultaneously. Decision-making slows. The period between recognising a crisis and people acting effectively is typically 2–5 minutes of confusion — the window where leadership matters most.
The most dangerous minute in any maritime emergency is the first one. Before anyone understands what is happening — before anyone has been told what to do — people are scanning each other for cues. If they see calm authority, they move toward adaptive behaviour. If they see confusion, they slide toward panic.
🚢
Passenger vs crew behaviourCrew have trained responses and understood roles — they regress less quickly. Passengers have no role and no training — they are entirely dependent on crew for direction. A single calm, authoritative crew member can influence the behaviour of 50+ passengers.
🧩
Group behaviour effectsPeople in groups look to others for behavioural cues — this is called social proof. In an emergency, the first person who runs can trigger a stampede. The first person who stops and looks calm can trigger calm. You — as an officer — are always the reference point in your zone.

Case Study — Real Incident
Costa Concordia — January 2012
The Costa Concordia struck a reef with 4,229 people aboard. The initial PA announcement said there was an "electrical problem." Passengers returned to their cabins. When the vessel began listing, passengers were unled, disorganised, and received contradictory instructions. Crew were not authorised to initiate evacuation without the Captain's order. The delay cost 32 lives.
⚠ Lesson: Early, truthful communication prevents panic. Ambiguous communication causes it. Officers with designated duties must act without waiting for permission when there is an immediate threat to life.

Leadership in Crisis
Authority without clarity fails. Clarity without authority fails. You need both — simultaneously.
▶ READ
🗣️
Projecting authorityAuthority in a crisis is created through posture, voice, and certainty of action — not rank. A junior officer who moves with purpose and speaks clearly will be followed. A senior officer who appears uncertain will be ignored. Physical presence, eye contact, and a firm voice are tools — use them.
🎭
Managing your own fearYou will be afraid. That is normal and useful — it sharpens attention. The skill is not eliminating fear but preventing it from showing in your voice and movement. Slow your breathing deliberately. Speak slightly slower than feels natural. This is trainable — drills are the training ground.
🔄
Delegating in crisisYou cannot manage a muster station alone. Identify capable people immediately — including passengers — and give them specific tasks. "You — stand at that door and let no one past." A specific instruction to a named or pointed-at person is followed. A general call for help is usually ignored.
📊
Maintaining situation awarenessYour primary job as a crisis leader is knowing what is happening — not doing tasks yourself. Keep looking around. Keep receiving reports. Keep updating your mental picture. A leader who gets absorbed in a single task loses the overview and loses control of the situation.
The leadership paradox in crisis: You must project certainty while operating with incomplete information. You will not know everything. You must act anyway — clearly, calmly, and with the best information you have at that moment. Waiting for certainty is waiting too long.

Scenario — Muster station chaos

You are the muster station officer for Station 3. The general alarm has sounded. 180 passengers are arriving at your station simultaneously. Several are shouting. Three are trying to get back to their cabins. One woman has collapsed. The gangway area is becoming blocked. Your headset is not working.

You have 30 seconds before this becomes a stampede. What do you do FIRST?
You have established vocal authority. The three people trying to leave are still pushing back. The blockage is growing. The woman who collapsed needs attention. You cannot do both simultaneously. What do you prioritise?
The blockage is clearing. A male passenger — well-built and clearly experienced — steps forward and says "I know first aid — let me through." What do you do?
Case Study — Real Incident
MV Estonia — September 1994
852 people died when the Estonia sank in the Baltic. Survivors reported that the officers who survived were those who gave direct, clear, repeated instructions. Crew who stood watching were not followed. Crew who moved with purpose and pointed people toward lifeboats were followed — even by people who had never met them before.
⚠ Lesson: In a crisis, people do not follow rank — they follow visible purposeful action. An officer who moves with intent, points clearly, and repeats instructions is the most powerful leadership tool available.

Crowd Management
Moving large numbers of people safely through a vessel under crisis conditions
▶ READ
🚶
Flow and bottlenecksCrowds naturally flow like liquid — they fill available space and slow at narrowings. Know where the bottlenecks are on your vessel: stairwells, doorways, muster station entrances. Station crew at every bottleneck — not at the ends. One crew member at a 90cm doorway can control 500 people.
🗣️
Directing movementPoint physically. Say "Move to the left — keep walking — stay together." Repeat, loudly, continuously. Do not give complex instructions. People in crowds process simple directional commands. "Go to the forward muster station" is too complex. "Forward — go forward — that way" is correct.
🧘
Calming techniquesUse the STOP — BREATHE — LISTEN sequence. Ask people to stop moving for 10 seconds, take a breath, and listen to you. Even one calm voice in a crowd disrupts the panic feedback loop. Acknowledge fear directly: "I know you are scared — I need you to walk calmly to be safe."
♿
Vulnerable personsIdentify and separately direct people who cannot move at the crowd's pace: elderly, disabled, unaccompanied children, people in shock. They become hazards if left in the crowd flow. Assign a crew member or capable passenger to each — do not attempt to manage them yourself.
Never allow backward flow in a crowd. People moving against the crowd direction at a bottleneck cause crushes. Physically block reverse movement. Use your body, your voice, and other crew. A crowd crush is fatal within 90 seconds of full compression.

Emergency Communication
What you say, how you say it, and when — in the order that keeps people alive
▶ READ
📻
PA broadcast structure — NASSNotice (attention signal), Announcement (what is happening), Specific instruction (what to do), Supervision (who will help). Every emergency broadcast must contain all four elements in this order — nothing else.
🔊
Voice — rate, tone, volumeSpeak at 60–70% of normal speed. Slightly lower pitch than normal. Volume should fill the space. A shouted message is not processed as clearly as a firm, measured one. Test your PA voice in drills — not in the emergency itself.
🌍
Multilingual considerationsOn international vessels, key emergency instructions must be given in the working language AND in any languages spoken by a significant proportion of passengers (typically English + local language). Simple sentences — active voice — no idioms. "Go to Deck 5" not "Proceed to the assembly point."
🤫
What NOT to sayNever use the words "fire," "sinking," "abandon," or "evacuate" before the situation requires it — these words trigger immediate panic responses regardless of context. Use: "precautionary muster," "safety drill," "assembly required." Give direction, not diagnosis.
The Costa Concordia lesson on communication: The initial PA said there was an "electrical problem." Passengers returned to their cabins. Had the announcement directed people to muster stations immediately — even with a vague reason — hundreds more would have survived. Directing to muster does not require explaining the emergency.

Decision-Making Under Time Pressure
No perfect information, no time to think — you must decide anyway
▶ READ
🧭
Recognition-Primed Decision (RPD)Experienced commanders do not compare options — they pattern-match. They see a situation, recognise it as a type they know, and act on the first option that seems workable. This is faster and more reliable under pressure than formal analysis. Drills build the pattern library.
🚫
Analysis paralysisThe attempt to gather all information before deciding. In a fast-moving crisis, this results in no decision being made until the decision has been made by events. A 70% solution now beats a 100% solution in five minutes. Decide, act, observe, adjust.
🏁
The FORDEC modelFacts → Options → Risks → Decide → Execute → Check. Use this when you have 60+ seconds. In a fast crisis, compress to: What do I know? What are my options? Decide. Act. Check. If it is working, continue. If not, adjust immediately.
👂
Using crew informationYour crew sees things you do not. Create a fast information loop: brief updates, no narrative, action-focused. "Fire is spreading — starboard side" not "I was down on Deck 4 and I could see that the fire which started near the..." Interrupt if necessary. You need data, not stories.
Sunk cost in crisis: Once you have committed a course of action, you will feel pressure to continue even when it is not working — because changing feels like admitting failure. Recognise this. If what you are doing is not working, stop and adjust. In a crisis, changing a bad decision is leadership, not weakness.

Post-Crisis Management
When the emergency is over, the work is not — welfare, reporting, and learning come next
▶ READ
🫂
Immediate welfareAfter the crisis event, crew and passengers may be in shock, injured, or acutely distressed. Physical needs first: warmth, water, medical attention. Then emotional acknowledgement: briefly and directly confirm that people are safe and what happens next. Do not minimise what happened.
📝
Contemporaneous reportingWrite your log as soon as physically possible after the event — times, observations, decisions, communications, and actions taken. Memory degrades rapidly under post-crisis stress. A log written four hours later will be significantly less accurate than one written at 30 minutes.
🔒
Preserving evidenceDo not move, clean, alter, or dispose of anything in the area relevant to the event until officially instructed. Flag state investigators and P&I club surveyors need the scene as it was. Instruct crew explicitly — well-meaning cleaning destroys evidence.
🧠
Psychological first aidOfficers are not immune to crisis stress — neither are they immune to post-traumatic stress. Check on your crew in the 24–48 hours after a serious event. Normalise reactions. Make the ship's doctor or counselling services available. Watch for signs of acute stress: withdrawal, hyper-alertness, inability to sleep.
Post-event investigation is learning, not blame. The goal of a post-crisis investigation is to understand what happened and prevent recurrence — not to assign individual fault. Approach it with that mindset. If you made a decision that did not work out — document it honestly. The system learns from your honesty.
