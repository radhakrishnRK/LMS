Module 1: What Constitutes a Change?

A 'change' requiring MOC is any modification to the vessel, crew, procedures, or operations that introduces uncertainty and increases accident risk. Common changes include:

  • Crew Changes: New officers, ratings, or specialists joining the vessel
  • New Equipment: Installation of new machinery, navigation systems, or safety equipment
  • New Procedures: Changes to SMS procedures, emergency procedures, or operational practices
  • New Trading Areas: Operating in new geographic regions with different regulations or hazards
  • SMS Revisions: Updates to the Safety Management System
  • Maintenance Bypasses: Temporary repairs or deferred maintenance affecting operations

Each of these changes introduces uncertainty and requires a structured MOC process to manage risk.

Module 2: Why MOC is Needed

Change increases accident risk because it introduces uncertainty. When crew members don't understand a change or aren't prepared for it, they make mistakes. MOC is needed to:

  • Identify Hazards: Understand what new risks the change introduces
  • Assess Impact: Evaluate how the change affects operations, safety, and compliance
  • Communicate: Ensure all crew members understand the change and their responsibilities
  • Prepare: Provide training and resources needed for the change
  • Monitor: Track the change implementation and identify problems early

Maritime accidents are often caused by unmanaged change. A crew member joins without proper familiarisation, a new procedure isn't communicated, or equipment is installed without training.

Module 3: The MOC Process

The MOC process is a structured approach to managing change safely:

  • Step 1 - Identify the Change: Clearly define what is changing and why
  • Step 2 - Assess Risk: Identify hazards and potential consequences
  • Step 3 - Communicate: Inform all affected crew members and stakeholders
  • Step 4 - Update Procedures: Revise relevant SMS procedures and work instructions
  • Step 5 - Provide Training: Ensure crew has the knowledge and skills needed
  • Step 6 - Implement: Execute the change with appropriate controls
  • Step 7 - Monitor: Track implementation and identify issues early
  • Step 8 - Review: Assess effectiveness and make adjustments if needed

This systematic approach ensures that changes are managed safely and that crew members are prepared.

Module 4: Crew Change & Implementation

Crew change is the most frequent MOC event onboard. When new crew members join, they introduce uncertainty because they:

  • Don't know the vessel's layout, systems, and procedures
  • May not be familiar with the company's SMS
  • May have different training backgrounds and experience levels
  • May speak different languages or have different cultural backgrounds

The MOC process for crew change includes:

  • Structured Familiarisation: Systematic introduction to the vessel and procedures
  • Handover Period: Overlap between old and new crew members
  • Mentoring: Senior officers guide new crew members
  • Competence Verification: Confirm new crew members can perform their duties
  • Sign-Off: Document completion of familiarisation and competence

Module 5: Documentation & Case Studies

MOC Documentation: SIRE inspectors look for evidence that MOC was managed properly:

  • MOC forms or records identifying the change
  • Risk assessments documenting hazards and controls
  • Communication records showing crew was informed
  • Updated procedures and work instructions
  • Training records confirming crew competence
  • Implementation records showing the change was executed
  • Monitoring and review records

Case Study - Unmanaged Equipment Change: A vessel installed new cargo handling equipment but didn't provide training. During the first operation, the crew made mistakes, damaging cargo and injuring a crew member. The accident investigation found that MOC was not followed.

Case Study - Unmanaged Crew Change: A new Chief Officer joined without proper familiarisation. During a cargo operation, he made a critical error because he didn't understand the vessel's procedures. The near-miss was prevented by the quick action of an experienced crew member.
