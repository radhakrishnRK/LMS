📌 Module 1: Mentoring Fundamentals

Directing, Coaching & Mentoring

Understanding the Three Approaches

Directing

Telling someone what to do and how to do it. Appropriate for urgent situations or when the person lacks basic knowledge. Example: "Stop! The valve is leaking. Close it immediately using the red handle."

Coaching

Guiding someone through a task by asking questions and providing guidance. Appropriate when they have some knowledge but need development. Example: "What do you think caused the valve to leak? What would you do to fix it?"

Mentoring

Supporting someone's long-term development through regular conversations, feedback, and advocacy. Appropriate for developing future leaders. Example: "Over the next month, I want you to lead the maintenance checks. I'll observe and we'll discuss your approach."

🤝 Module 2: What Junior Crew Need

Support, Challenge & Advocacy

The Four Pillars of Mentoring

1. Feedback

Regular, specific, and balanced feedback on performance. Junior crew need to know what they're doing well and where they can improve. Feedback should be timely and focused on behaviour, not personality.

2. Challenge

Stretching assignments that develop new skills and confidence. Junior crew need opportunities to try new things with your support. Challenge without support creates anxiety; support without challenge creates complacency.

3. Support

Being available when they struggle, believing in their potential, and helping them navigate difficulties. Support means listening without judgment and providing guidance when needed.

4. Advocacy

Speaking on their behalf, recognizing their achievements, and opening doors for their development. Advocacy means ensuring they get fair opportunities and that their contributions are recognized.

💬 Module 3: Structuring Mentoring Conversations

Goal, Observation, Feedback, Action

The GOFA Framework

G - Goal

Start by clarifying the purpose of the conversation. "I want to discuss your approach to the engine room watch handover." This sets expectations and helps the junior officer understand why you're having the conversation.

O - Observation

Share specific observations of their behaviour or performance. "I noticed you didn't check the fuel tank level during your handover yesterday." Focus on what you observed, not assumptions.

F - Feedback

Provide balanced feedback. "This is important because fuel tank monitoring is a critical safety check. You've been strong in other areas of your watch routine." Acknowledge what they're doing well while addressing the gap.

A - Action

Agree on specific actions for improvement. "Next watch, I'd like you to walk me through your fuel monitoring procedure. We'll identify what you need to remember." Make it collaborative, not punitive.

🎯 Module 4: Feedback Skills

Giving & Receiving Feedback

Effective Feedback Principles

Specific

Instead of "You need to improve your communication," say "During the morning briefing, you didn't ask clarifying questions about the cargo operation. I'd like to see you engage more actively."

Behavioural

Focus on what they did, not who they are. "You interrupted the Chief Engineer three times" is better than "You're disrespectful." Behavioural feedback is actionable.

Timely

Give feedback soon after the event, while it's fresh. Waiting weeks reduces its impact. However, if emotions are high, wait for calm before discussing.

Balanced

Include what they did well alongside areas for improvement. "Your technical knowledge is excellent. I'd like to see you develop your communication skills further."

Receiving Feedback

Listen without defensiveness. Ask clarifying questions. Thank them for the feedback. This models the behaviour you want from junior crew and strengthens your relationship.

🆘 Module 5: Supporting Crew in Difficulty

Development, Challenges & TRB Sign-off

Mentoring Beyond Performance

Recognizing Crew in Difficulty

Watch for signs of struggle: withdrawn behaviour, reduced performance, increased errors, or personal issues affecting work. Early intervention prevents problems from escalating.

Creating a Safe Space

Junior crew need to feel they can approach you with problems. "My door is always open" means nothing if your actions show otherwise. Be approachable, non-judgmental, and confidential.

TRB Task Sign-off as Mentoring

Don't just sign the TRB form. Use it as a mentoring moment. Discuss what they learned, what was challenging, and how they'll apply this knowledge. This reinforces learning and shows you care about their development.

Your Role in Developing Future Leaders

As a senior officer, you're not just managing today's operations. You're developing tomorrow's Masters and Chief Engineers. Invest in mentoring. It's your responsibility to the maritime industry.
