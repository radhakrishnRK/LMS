Module 1 of 6 · STCW tanker operations

Pump Room as a High-Risk Space

Understand why the pump room is treated differently from ordinary machinery spaces.

Training briefing
Pump rooms are enclosed spaces with cargo pipelines, pumps, stripping lines, bilges and drain arrangements. Vapour may accumulate at low levels because many petroleum vapours are heavier than air.
The main hazards are toxic gas, flammable vapour, oxygen deficiency, rotating machinery, hot surfaces, slippery ladders, poor visibility and delayed rescue access.
Pump-room access is not routine walking around. It is a controlled entry requiring permission, communication and atmosphere awareness.
Stop point: Any unexpected gas reading, loss of communication, cargo leak, ventilation failure or alarm is a reason to stop the job and escalate.

Module 2 of 6 · STCW tanker operations

Toxic Gas, Flammable Vapour and Oxygen Deficiency

Read the atmosphere before the atmosphere reads you.

Training briefing
Atmosphere testing should normally include oxygen, percentage of LEL, hydrogen sulphide and carbon monoxide where applicable.
A safe oxygen reading is normally about 20.9 percent. Oxygen below safe limits means no entry unless an approved rescue or emergency procedure with breathing apparatus is used.
LEL readings indicate flammable vapour risk. Any unexpected rise must be treated as serious and escalated immediately.
H₂S can disable the sense of smell. Never rely on odour to decide whether the space is safe.
Stop point: Any unexpected gas reading, loss of communication, cargo leak, ventilation failure or alarm is a reason to stop the job and escalate.

Module 3 of 6 · STCW tanker operations

Ventilation, Entry Control and Permit-to-Work

No entry without control, no control without evidence.

Training briefing
Ventilation must be effective at low levels where vapours accumulate. The pump-room exhaust and supply arrangements must be understood by watchkeepers.
Permit-to-work should define the job, persons entering, time limit, atmosphere checks, ventilation status, communication method, rescue arrangements and stop-work triggers.
A standby person outside the entry point must maintain contact and must not enter for rescue unless properly equipped and authorised.
Stop point: Any unexpected gas reading, loss of communication, cargo leak, ventilation failure or alarm is a reason to stop the job and escalate.

Module 4 of 6 · STCW tanker operations

Machinery, Slips, Noise and Work Inside the Pump Room

The atmosphere is not the only hazard.

Training briefing
Cargo pumps and stripping pumps involve rotating parts, pressure, vibration and noise. Guards must be in place and abnormal sound, smell, heat or vibration must be reported.
Slippery gratings, ladder falls and poor lighting are common risks. Maintain three points of contact and do not carry unnecessary loose items.
Do not open drains, loosen flanges or adjust pump systems unless the job is authorised, isolated and covered by the correct permit.
Stop point: Any unexpected gas reading, loss of communication, cargo leak, ventilation failure or alarm is a reason to stop the job and escalate.

Module 5 of 6 · STCW tanker operations

Monitoring, Communication and Watchkeeping

A safe entry depends on people outside the space as much as the person inside.

Training briefing
Before entry, agree the communication method: radio, sound-powered phone, fixed intercom, visual check or a combination as per ship procedure.
The standby person must know the names of entrants, expected duration, rescue arrangements and emergency contact method.
Fixed gas alarms, bilge alarms, pump alarms and ventilation alarms must be taken seriously. Alarm acknowledgement is not the same as problem resolution.
Stop point: Any unexpected gas reading, loss of communication, cargo leak, ventilation failure or alarm is a reason to stop the job and escalate.

Module 6 of 6 · STCW tanker operations

Emergency Response and Rescue from the Pump Room

The first rescuer must not become the second casualty.

Training briefing
If a person collapses in the pump room, the immediate actions are raise alarm, inform bridge or cargo control room, stop cargo operations if required, muster rescue team and prepare breathing apparatus.
Unprotected rescue attempts in enclosed spaces often create multiple casualties. Rescue must be carried out with SCBA, lifelines, communications and supervision.
After rescue, provide first aid, oxygen or CPR as appropriate, medical advice, exposure documentation and incident reporting. Preserve evidence and review controls.
Stop point: Any unexpected gas reading, loss of communication, cargo leak, ventilation failure or alarm is a reason to stop the job and escalate.
