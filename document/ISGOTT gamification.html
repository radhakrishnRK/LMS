Module 1 · ISGOTT Ch. 1–2
Properties of Petroleum Cargoes
Flammability, toxicity, static electricity — the science behind tanker safety rules
▶ READ — ISGOTT Ch. 1
Property Definition ISGOTT significance
Flash point §1.3 Minimum temp at which liquid produces ignitable vapour Determines hazard category. Below 60°C = highly flammable. Crude oil may be as low as −30°C.
Flammable range §1.4 LEL to UEL — concentrations that will ignite Crude oil: LEL ≈1%, UEL ≈10%. Inside this range, any ignition source causes explosion.
Vapour density §1.5 Weight of vapour relative to air (air = 1) Petroleum vapour >1. Sinks to lowest points. Pump rooms, bilges, cofferdams — always test first.
Auto-ignition temp §1.6 Temp at which vapour ignites without a spark Typically 250–450°C. Hot engine surfaces, exhaust pipes, and steam lines are potential auto-ignition sources.
Boiling point / vapour pressure §1.7 Temp at which liquid becomes gas; pressure of vapour at liquid surface High vapour pressure cargoes produce more vapour at ambient temp. Higher risk of flammable atmosphere.
H₂S content §1.11 Hydrogen sulphide dissolved in crude or fuel oil OEL 1 ppm. IDLH 100 ppm. Paralyses olfactory nerve at dangerous concentrations. Always gas-test.
Static electricity §1.14 Charge generated by cargo movement through pipes/filters Splash loading, free fall, filter separators, and mist generate static. Bonding and relaxation time are controls.
📊
Flammable range — the kill zonePetroleum atmosphere in the range between LEL and UEL will explode when ignited. Above UEL: too rich — no ignition but also asphyxiation hazard. Below LEL: safe to work. The IGS goal is to stay above UEL by keeping O₂ below 8%.
⚡
Static — the invisible hazardPetroleum movement generates electrostatic charge. This discharges as a spark. ISGOTT requires: bonding cable ship-to-shore, reduced flow rate at start of loading, 30-minute relaxation time before gauging or sampling.
☠️
H₂S — smell is not a detectorH₂S smells of rotten eggs at low ppm. At >150 ppm it immediately anaesthetises the olfactory nerve — you smell nothing at a concentration that is lethal within minutes. The only reliable detector is an instrument. Never rely on smell in a cargo area.
🌡️
Auto-ignition without a sparkIf a cargo vapour contacts a surface hotter than its auto-ignition temperature, it ignites without a flame or spark. Hot exhaust pipes, boiler casings, and steam lines must be insulated and kept away from cargo areas.
ISGOTT §1.14.3 — 30-minute relaxation rule: After loading, allow 30 minutes before opening any tank fitting for gauging, sampling, or temperature measurement. Static generated during loading dissipates during this period. Breaching this rule has caused tanker fires.

Module 2 · ISGOTT Ch. 3
Ignition Source Control
Every ISGOTT ignition control rule exists because a specific incident proved what happens when it is broken
▶ READ — ISGOTT Ch. 3
🚬
Smoking §3.4Designated smoking room only. Self-closing metal door. Fitted ashtray. E-cigarettes have same rules as tobacco. No smoking on deck, in cargo areas, in pump rooms, or in accommodation outside the designated room. No exceptions — ever.
🔧
Hot work §3.6Welding, burning, grinding, drilling — any work producing heat or spark. Requires: hot work permit signed by Master, confirmed gas-free atmosphere, continuous gas monitoring during work, fire watch for one hour after. No hot work within 30m of open cargo tanks.
📱
Electrical equipment §3.8Only IS (intrinsically safe) or Ex (explosion-proof) rated equipment in hazardous areas. Mobile phones, personal radios, cameras, ordinary torches, hearing aids with batteries — all prohibited on deck during cargo operations. Check for IS marking before use.
⚙️
Static electricity controls §3.9Bonding wire connected before manifold connection and disconnected after. Initial loading rate ≤1m/s until ship's bottom covered. 30-minute relaxation before opening tanks after loading. No free-fall loading. No non-conductive hoses unless specially rated.
🌩️
Electrical storms §3.11Stop cargo operations when lightning within 5nm. Close all tank openings. Wait until storm passes. Lightning can induce static discharge in vapour atmosphere. Do not restart until storm has cleared the area.
🛞
Galley fires and boilers §3.5Galley flues, boiler uptakes, and steam system insulation are potential ignition sources. All openings serving cargo areas must be closed. Ash and clinker from boilers: cool completely before disposal, never near cargo areas.
ISGOTT §3.8.2 — The phone rule: There is no exception for a "quick call" on the cargo deck. A mobile phone can generate a spark when receiving a call, when a message vibrates, or when the battery connection shifts. Officers who have permitted this on tankers have caused explosions.

Module 3 · ISGOTT Ch. 4–5
Cargo Operations Safety
Vapour control, loading, discharge, and tank cleaning — the operations where most incidents occur
▶ READ — ISGOTT Ch. 4–5
💨
Vapour emission control §4.2Closing all non-operating tank openings before cargo operations is mandatory. Vapour venting must be through pressure/vacuum (P/V) valves only. P/V valves prevent excess pressure (overpressure) and vacuum (implode risk). Never block or bypass a P/V valve.
⬆️
Loading §4.3Start at ≤1m/s until ship's bottom covered (typically 1–1.5m depth). Rate can increase after this. Final ullage must allow for thermal expansion. Stop loading 30 minutes before ullage/temperature measurement. Monitor trim and list throughout.
⬇️
Discharge §4.4Confirm IGS in service before opening tank valves. Monitor tank pressure — maintain positive pressure. Stop pumps before tanks empty to avoid air ingress and vapour release. Stripping: slow, controlled, with gas monitoring.
🫧
Tank cleaning §5.1Most dangerous cargo operation. Requires: gas-free or inerted atmosphere confirmed, washing machines operating within design parameters, return lines clear, no personnel inside. COW (crude oil washing) requires special procedures and dedicated COW manual on board.
🔬
Gauging and sampling §4.6Portable equipment: IS or intrinsically safe only. 30-minute relaxation after loading. Closed gauging preferred — open gauging only with full PPE and gas-free atmosphere confirmed. Sampling containers must be clean and properly sealed.
⚖️
Trim, stability and stress §4.8Loading plan must be calculated before operations. Free surface effect from part-filled tanks reduces GM. Maximum stress and shear force limits must not be exceeded during loading or discharge sequences. Always sequence by loading plan.
ISGOTT §5.1.5 — Tank entry during cleaning: Crew must never enter a tank during or immediately after washing. Mechanical agitation from washing machines creates a mist. The mist is both flammable and toxic. The atmosphere is not safe even if pre-washing tests read acceptable.

Module 4 · ISGOTT Ch. 6
Inert Gas System (IGS)
The IGS is the primary safety system on crude tankers — understanding its limits is as important as understanding its purpose
▶ READ — ISGOTT Ch. 6
🎯
Purpose §6.1Maintain cargo tank atmosphere above the upper explosive limit (UEL) by keeping O₂ below 8% by volume. At this O₂ level, no mixture of petroleum vapour and gas will ignite regardless of vapour concentration. IGS does not eliminate vapour — it eliminates the conditions for ignition.
🔩
IGS sources §6.2Flue gas (from boiler/main engine exhaust) — scrubbed and cooled. Nitrogen generator (inert gas generator/IGG). Shore supply (rarely). Flue gas contains CO₂ (12–14%), N₂, CO, and SOx — all of which need scrubbing to remove acid gases and soot before entering tanks.
⚠️
Critical limits §6.4O₂ content of supplied IG: max 5% (ideal <2%). Tank atmosphere: maintain below 8% O₂. If tank O₂ rises above 8%: stop operations, investigate cause, re-inert before continuing. Do not discharge with tank O₂ above 8%.
🔄
IGS deck water seal / non-return §6.3The deck water seal prevents backflow of cargo vapour into the IGS plant if pressure is lost. Must be maintained at correct water level at all times. If the water seal fails, cargo vapour can enter the IGS plant — risk of explosion in the plant itself.
🚨
IGS failures §6.6High O₂ alarm: stop operations immediately. Pressure failure: close all tank valves. Scrubber failure: secure IGS. If positive pressure cannot be maintained: do not open tanks. Document all IGS failures in the cargo log and notify the Master.
📊
Purging and gas-freeing §6.8Purging: replacing cargo vapour with IG until below LEL throughout. Gas-freeing: replacing IG with fresh air after purging — ventilate until O₂ >20.9% and hydrocarbon <1% LEL throughout tank. Only after purging — never directly from inerted/cargo-laden to gas-free.
ISGOTT §6.8.2 — Never gas-free directly: A cargo tank must be purged with inert gas below LEL throughout before any gas-freeing ventilation begins. Introducing fresh air into a tank still containing cargo vapour creates a flammable atmosphere — exactly the condition the IGS was designed to prevent.

Module 5 · ISGOTT Ch. 7
Ship-Shore Safety Interface
SSSCL, hose connections, ESD, communication — the interface where responsibility transitions and incidents happen
▶ READ — ISGOTT Ch. 7
📋
SSSCL — purpose §7.2Ship-Shore Safety Checklist. A bilateral safety agreement — not a formality. Jointly signed. Confirms: communications established, ESD procedure agreed, manifold connections checked, cargo limits agreed, emergency procedures understood by both parties. Invalid if signed without being completed.
🔴
Emergency Shutdown (ESD) §7.4Either party can call ESD at any time for any safety reason. Response: stop all pumps immediately, close all manifold valves, inform the other party. The ESD procedure, signal, and responsibility must be agreed in the SSSCL before operations begin. Test the ESD before first cargo transfer.
🔗
Hose / loading arm connections §7.5All connections must be properly made with full gaskets and bolts. Test at 1.5× working pressure before transferring cargo. Check for leaks at manifold flanges at start of transfer and periodically throughout. Bonding wire connected before any connection is made.
📡
Communications §7.3Agree VHF channel in SSSCL. Maintain continuous watch — nominated officer, not delegated to rating. Emergency signal agreed (usually 3 short blasts plus VHF). If communications fail, stop cargo operations until restored. Never rely solely on shore communications equipment.
ISGOTT §7.2.4 — Shared responsibility: The SSSCL is signed by the officer responsible on both ship and terminal. Both parties are legally responsible for all items on the checklist. "The terminal signed it" is not a defence if a ship's item was not actually verified.

Module 6 · ISGOTT Ch. 9–10 · MARPOL Annex I
Emergency Response and MARPOL Compliance
Three cargo emergencies. MARPOL obligations. Real-time decisions — know them before you need them.
▶ READ — ISGOTT Ch. 9 + MARPOL Annex I
Emergency ISGOTT first action Do NOT
🔥 Cargo fire at manifold §9.2 Stop cargo (ESD). Close all valves. Notify terminal. Sound alarm. Muster fire party. Apply fixed foam if available. Prepare to disconnect hoses only if safe. Use water jet on burning oil (spreads fire). Disconnect hoses while cargo flows. Re-open valves once stopped.
🛢️ Cargo spill on deck §9.3 Stop cargo immediately. Notify terminal. Close all scuppers. Contain spill — absorbents, booms. Remove all ignition sources. Report under MARPOL. Photograph evidence. Open scuppers to drain overboard (MARPOL violation). Approach without PPE. Ignore small spills — all spills must be reported.
☠️ H₂S gas release §9.4 Sound alarm. Do NOT enter without SCBA. Account for all personnel. Evacuate affected area. Notify terminal. TMAS for casualties. Post guards — no entry without BA. Enter area without SCBA. Wait for the smell to confirm clearance. Try to ventilate before accounting for all personnel.
🌊 Man overboard during cargo ops §9.5 Stop cargo operations (ESD). Implement MOB procedure. Notify terminal and MRCC. Do not resume cargo until crew is accounted for or search is complete. Continue cargo operations while a person is in the water. Resume operations without confirming all personnel are aboard.
⚖️
MARPOL Annex I — key rules for tankersZero discharge of cargo oil or oily water overboard in port and restricted areas. Discharge at sea: only >50nm from land, vessel underway, <30 litres per nautical mile, and via approved OWS for bilge water. Cargo residues: never overboard — retain on board or to shore reception facility.
📓
Oil Record Book (ORB) — Part II MARPOLAll cargo and ballast operations must be recorded in ORB Part II. Including: loading, discharge, tank cleaning, ballasting/deballasting of cargo tanks, disposition of cargo residues. False entries are a criminal offence. ORB Part II is inspected at every port state control.
MARPOL — no exceptions for accidental spills: All overboard discharges of oil — including accidental — must be recorded in the ORB and reported to the port state and flag state. Attempting to conceal a spill by falsifying records or failing to report is a criminal offence with potential imprisonment for the Master.
