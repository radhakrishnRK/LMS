Module 1: Lookout Duties

What to look for, how to report, verbal vs alarm signals

The Lookout Responsibility

The lookout is one of the most critical duties assigned to a deck rating. Under COLREGS Rule 5, every vessel must at all times maintain a proper lookout by sight and hearing as well as by all available means appropriate to the prevailing circumstances.

Key Principle: The lookout is the eyes and ears of the vessel. A dedicated lookout must give their full attention to keeping a proper lookout and must not undertake any other duties that could interfere with this responsibility.

As a lookout, you must be able to identify and report:

  • Other vessels — their lights, shapes, bearing, and whether the bearing is changing
  • Navigation marks — buoys, lighthouses, beacons
  • Land features — coastline, headlands, breakwaters
  • Hazards — floating objects, debris, fishing nets, ice
  • Weather changes — fog, rain, squalls, visibility reduction
  • Distress signals — flares, lights, sound signals

How to Report Sightings

Reporting must be clear, concise, and immediate. The standard reporting format is:

ElementExample
Object type"Light", "Vessel", "Buoy", "Object in water"
Bearing"Two points on the port bow", "Red 045", "Dead ahead"
Additional info"Flashing", "Closing", "Stationary", "Bearing steady"
Important: Never assume the OOW has already seen what you have spotted. Always report every sighting — even if you think it is obvious. A bearing that is not changing indicates risk of collision.

Verbal vs Alarm Signals:

  • Verbal report — Standard method during normal watchkeeping. Report directly to the OOW on the bridge.
  • Sound-powered telephone — Used when the lookout is stationed on the forecastle or away from the bridge.
  • Alarm signal — Used in emergency situations only (e.g., man overboard, imminent collision risk). The general alarm or MOB alarm may be activated.

Lookout Discipline

Effective lookout requires discipline and focus:

  • No distractions — No mobile phones, no reading, no conversations unrelated to duty
  • Night vision — Allow 20-30 minutes for eyes to adapt to darkness. Avoid looking at bright lights.
  • Systematic scanning — Scan from one side to the other methodically, not randomly
  • Hearing — Listen for fog signals, engine sounds, breaking waves on shoals
  • Relief — Report all contacts and conditions to your relief when handing over
SIRE Inspector Note: SIRE inspectors may ask ratings directly: "What are your lookout duties?" and "How do you report a sighting?" You must be able to answer confidently.

Module 2: Helmsman Duties

Understanding helm orders, responses, and autopilot/manual changeover

The Helmsman Role

The helmsman (or quartermaster) is responsible for steering the vessel as directed by the OOW or the pilot. This is a critical safety role — incorrect helm action can lead to collision or grounding.

Key Principle: The helmsman must repeat every helm order back to the OOW before executing it. This is called closed-loop communication and ensures the order has been correctly understood.

Standard Helm Orders and Responses

Order GivenHelmsman ResponseAction
"Starboard 10""Starboard 10, Sir/Ma'am"Apply 10 degrees starboard helm
"Midships""Midships, Sir/Ma'am"Return rudder to zero
"Steady""Steady on [heading], Sir/Ma'am"Hold the current heading
"Steady as she goes""Steady as she goes, heading [xxx]"Maintain the heading at time of order
"Hard a-starboard""Hard a-starboard, Sir/Ma'am"Apply maximum starboard helm
"Port 20""Port 20, Sir/Ma'am"Apply 20 degrees port helm
"Ease to 5""Ease to 5, Sir/Ma'am"Reduce helm angle to 5 degrees
"Nothing to port""Nothing to port, Sir/Ma'am"Do not allow the vessel to swing to port
Critical Rule: When the helmsman has achieved the ordered heading, they must report: "Course [heading], Sir/Ma'am." The helmsman must never leave the wheel without permission from the OOW.

Autopilot to Manual Changeover

Changeover from autopilot to manual steering must be practised and understood:

  • When to change over — In confined waters, heavy traffic, restricted visibility, or when ordered by the OOW
  • Procedure — OOW orders "Change to hand steering." Helmsman switches to manual, confirms rudder response, reports "Hand steering, Sir/Ma'am"
  • Testing — After changeover, test the rudder by applying small helm angles to confirm response
  • SOLAS requirement — Manual steering must be tested at least once per watch in areas where hand steering is likely to be required
SIRE Inspector Note: Inspectors may ask the helmsman: "How do you change from autopilot to manual steering?" and "What helm orders do you know?" Be prepared to demonstrate.

Module 3: Mooring Station Duties

Line handling, winch operation, communication with bridge, personal safety

Mooring Operations — The Rating's Role

Mooring operations are among the most dangerous activities onboard. Parted mooring lines have caused fatalities. Every rating must understand their role and the hazards involved.

Safety Critical: Mooring lines under tension store enormous energy. A parted line can recoil with lethal force. Always stay out of the snap-back zone and never stand in the bight of a line.

Line Handling Duties

  • Know your lines — Head line, stern line, breast line, spring line — know which is which and their purpose
  • Heaving line — Throw accurately to shore. Never wrap the heaving line around your hand.
  • Tending lines — Keep lines taut as ordered. Slack lines can part under sudden load.
  • Securing on bitts — Use proper figure-of-eight turns. Ensure sufficient turns for holding power.
  • Synthetic lines — More dangerous than wire when they part — they snap back with extreme force

Winch Operation Safety

  • Training required — Never operate a winch without proper training and authorisation
  • Hands clear — Keep hands, fingers, and loose clothing away from the drum
  • Communication — Maintain clear communication with the mooring team leader and bridge
  • Emergency stop — Know the location and operation of the emergency stop on every winch
  • Brake operation — Understand brake application — never release a brake under load without orders
Bridge Communication: The bridge controls the operation. All line handling actions must be coordinated with bridge orders. Use radio or hand signals as established in the toolbox talk.

Personal Safety at Mooring Stations

  • PPE — Hard hat, safety shoes, gloves, high-visibility vest
  • Snap-back zones — Marked on deck. Never stand in a snap-back zone.
  • Bight of a line — Never stand inside the bight of a line being worked
  • Fairleads and rollers — Keep clear of lines running through fairleads
  • Fatigue — Report if you are too fatigued to work safely

Module 4: Gangway, Pilot Ladder & Cargo Area Safety

Rigging, safety nets, life rings, PPE, access restrictions, supervisor authority

Gangway Watch Duties

The gangway watchman is the first point of contact for anyone boarding the vessel. This is both a security and safety role.

  • Access control — Check identification of all visitors. Record names, company, time in/out.
  • Safety briefing — Ensure visitors receive a safety briefing before proceeding onboard
  • Gangway condition — Ensure the gangway is safe, properly rigged, well-lit, and the safety net is in place
  • Life ring — A life ring with light and line must be available at the gangway at all times
  • Communication — Maintain radio contact with the bridge/duty officer
ISPS Code: The gangway watch is part of the vessel's security plan. Unauthorised access must be prevented and reported.

Pilot Ladder Rigging

Pilot ladder rigging is a life-critical task. Improperly rigged pilot ladders have caused pilot deaths.

  • SOLAS Chapter V Regulation 23 — Specifies pilot ladder requirements
  • Condition — Steps must be level, evenly spaced, and in good condition
  • Securing — Ladder must be secured to the ship's hull, not to the rails
  • Lighting — Adequate lighting at the embarkation point
  • Safety net — Rigged beneath the pilot ladder access point
  • Life ring — Ready for immediate use at the pilot station
  • Weight placard — Maximum safe working load must be displayed
  • Manropes — If required, properly secured and of correct diameter

Cargo Area Safety for Ratings

Ratings must understand cargo area restrictions and follow supervisor authority at all times:

  • Access restrictions — Never enter the cargo area without permission from the duty officer
  • Enclosed spaces — Never enter an enclosed space without a valid entry permit and proper procedures
  • PPE requirements — Wear all required PPE as specified for the cargo area (hard hat, safety shoes, coveralls, gas detector as required)
  • Supervisor authority — Follow instructions from the officer in charge. If you are unsure, ask.
  • Stop Work Authority — Every crew member has the right and duty to stop work if they believe it is unsafe
Critical Safety Rule: Never enter a cargo tank, pump room, void space, or any enclosed space without following the enclosed space entry procedure. Failure to follow this rule has caused multiple fatalities in the maritime industry.

Module 5: Toolbox Talks & SIRE Interaction

Participation rights, raising concerns, and what inspectors ask ratings

Toolbox Talk Participation

A toolbox talk is a brief safety meeting held before every job. As a rating, your participation is not just expected — it is essential.

  • Listen actively — Understand the task, hazards, precautions, and your specific role
  • Ask questions — If anything is unclear, ask. There are no stupid questions when safety is involved.
  • Raise concerns — If you see a hazard that has not been mentioned, speak up
  • Sign the attendance sheet — Your signature confirms you understood the briefing
  • Right to refuse — If you do not understand the task or feel it is unsafe, you have the right to say so
Key Principle: A toolbox talk is not a one-way lecture. It is a two-way conversation. The supervisor briefs the team, and the team confirms understanding and raises concerns.

SIRE Inspector Interaction — What Ratings May Be Asked

Under SIRE 2.0, inspectors will speak directly to ratings. This is not something to fear — it is an opportunity to demonstrate your competence.

TopicTypical Questions
Familiarisation"When did you join? Were you familiarised? What did it cover?"
Emergency duties"What is your muster station? What are your emergency duties?"
Lookout"What are your lookout duties? How do you report a sighting?"
Mooring"What PPE do you wear? Where are the snap-back zones?"
Enclosed spaces"Would you enter a tank without a permit? What would you do if asked?"
Toolbox talks"Was a toolbox talk held before this job? What was discussed?"
Stop Work"Do you know your right to stop work? Have you ever used it?"
Safety equipment"Where is the nearest fire extinguisher? Life ring? SCBA?"
Preparation Tip: Know your emergency duties, your muster station, the location of safety equipment near your work area, and the basics of your familiarisation. Answer honestly — inspectors respect honest answers more than rehearsed ones.

Your Rights and Responsibilities

  • Right to a safe workplace — Under MLC 2006, every seafarer has the right to safe working conditions
  • Right to training — You must receive proper training before being assigned duties
  • Right to PPE — The company must provide appropriate PPE at no cost to you
  • Responsibility to follow procedures — Follow the SMS, wear PPE, attend toolbox talks
  • Responsibility to report — Report hazards, near-misses, and unsafe conditions
  • Responsibility to be fit for duty — Adequate rest, no alcohol or drugs on duty
