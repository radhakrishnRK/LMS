Module 1 · MARPOL Annex I Regulations 15–17
MARPOL Annex I — The Bilge Water Legal Framework
What the law actually says — the exact conditions under which overboard discharge is permitted and when it is criminal
▶ READ
💧
Sources of machinery space bilge waterBilge water accumulates from: engine cooling water leaks, stern tube seal leakage, bilge cleaning operations, fuel system drips, hydraulic system leaks, and condensation. It is an inevitable product of machinery operation — but how it is managed is a criminal matter. The quantity must be controlled and reduced, but cannot be zero.
🌍
MARPOL Annex I — the governing instrumentMARPOL 73/78 Annex I controls oil pollution from ships. Regulation 15 governs machinery space bilge water discharge. Regulation 17 requires the Oil Record Book (ORB). These regulations have the force of criminal law in every signatory state — which is virtually every maritime nation. "I didn't know" is not a defence.
MARPOL Annex I — Regulation 15.3 — Permitted Discharge Conditions
Discharge of bilge water from machinery spaces is only permitted when ALL of the following conditions are simultaneously met:

(a) The ship is proceeding en route — not at anchor, not manoeuvring, not in port
(b) The oil content of the effluent does not exceed 15 parts per million
(c) The ship is not within a Special Area (Baltic Sea, Black Sea, Antarctic Area, etc.)
(d) The ship has in operation an approved OWS with a 15ppm alarm device that automatically stops overboard discharge when oil content exceeds 15ppm

If ANY condition is not met — overboard discharge is prohibited under international law.
Special Areas — Zero Discharge
The following areas have zero discharge of machinery space bilge water: Baltic Sea, Black Sea, Red Sea, Gulfs Area (Persian/Arabian Gulf), Gulf of Aden, Antarctic Area, North West European Waters, Oman Area of Arabian Sea, Southern South African Waters.

In Special Areas: no overboard discharge is permitted under any circumstances — regardless of OWS output. All bilge water must be retained for port discharge.
⚠️
MARPOL Annex I enforcementUnlike many international conventions, MARPOL violations are prosecuted under the domestic criminal law of port states. USA, Australia, UK, EU member states all actively prosecute. Penalties range from company fines (millions of USD) to imprisonment of the Chief Engineer, Master, and — increasingly — the company's shore management. This is not administrative — it is criminal.
🔍
Why bilge management is the most inspected areaPSC inspectors, USCG, and SIRE inspectors give bilge management and ORB records more scrutiny than almost any other item. The OWS is operationally simple but the opportunities for illegal bypass are obvious — and the evidence trail (ORB records, OWS hour meters, bilge alarm logs, and witness testimony) is forensically examined in prosecutions.
If you are ever instructed to bypass the OWS, pump bilge overboard without the OWS running, or make false ORB entries — you must refuse. The instruction itself is criminal. You have the right and obligation to refuse an unlawful order. If the pressure continues, contact the company DPA. Your refusal is a legal defence. Compliance is not.

Module 2 · MARPOL Annex I Regulation 17 · ORB Part I
Oil Record Book — Mandatory Entries and Inspector Scrutiny
The ORB is the primary evidence in every MARPOL bilge prosecution — what it must contain and how investigators use it
▶ READ
📓
ORB Part I — machinery space operationsOil Record Book Part I records all machinery space bilge and oil operations. MARPOL Regulation 17 makes it mandatory. It must be: kept in the machinery space or bridge, available for inspection for 3 years after the last entry, signed after each completed operation by the officer in charge, and countersigned by the Master on each page.
✍️
Master signature obligationEvery page of the ORB Part I must be countersigned by the Master. This is not an administrative formality — it makes the Master personally accountable for the accuracy of the record. If an entry is false, the Master is criminally liable. The Master must actually review entries before signing — not rubber-stamp. Unsigned pages are a deficiency during PSC inspection.
🔍
How investigators read the ORBInvestigators cross-reference: (a) ORB discharge entries against (b) OWS hour meter readings, (c) bilge alarm records stored in the OWS memory, (d) sounding records for the bilge holding tank, and (e) crew testimony. Implausible volumes, impossible dates, entries that don't match alarm data, and erasures are all red flags. Modern OWS systems have tamper-evident memory logs that investigators can download.
⚡
Common ORB discrepancies that trigger prosecutionBilge quantities that do not decrease despite extended discharge records; OWS run times that cannot produce the recorded volume; alarm activations not recorded; bilge holding tank soundings inconsistent with recorded transfers; entries in pencil or white-out used; pages torn out; entries added later in different ink; dates out of sequence. Any of these in isolation may trigger investigation — several together will.

Contemporaneous means at the time — not at the end of the voyage. ORB entries must be made when the operation occurs. A Chief Engineer who fills in a week's worth of entries at the weekend to "keep the book tidy" has already violated MARPOL Regulation 17 — even if the operations themselves were compliant. The time of entry matters as evidence.

Module 3 · MARPOL Annex I Reg.16 · OWS Operation Manual
OWS Operation — 15ppm System, Alarm and Automatic Valve
The OWS is a precision instrument with specific operating requirements — understanding it prevents both environmental violations and false alarms
▶ READ
🔬
How the OWS worksOily water passes through a series of gravity separation, coalescing, and filtration stages. The separated oil is collected and returned to the bilge holding tank or dirty oil tank. The treated effluent passes through the 15ppm monitoring cell before being routed either overboard (if <15ppm) or back to the bilge tank (if ≥15ppm via the 3-way valve).
🚨
The 15ppm alarm and automatic shut-off valveThe 15ppm monitoring cell continuously measures oil content of treated effluent. When oil content reaches 15ppm, the alarm activates AND the 3-way automatic valve closes the overboard path and diverts effluent back to the bilge holding tank. This is automatic — the engineer on watch does not need to manually close the valve. The alarm record is stored in the OWS memory.
⚙️
Pre-operation checksBefore each OWS run: verify OWS alarm is functional (test lamp/test button); confirm ship is proceeding at sea (not anchored, not in port); confirm position is outside Special Areas; check bilge holding tank has capacity for return flow; confirm ORB is ready for contemporaneous entry; check OWS oil content meter calibration date is current.
🛑
When the 15ppm alarm firesThe 3-way valve automatically diverts effluent back to the bilge holding tank — the vessel is not illegally discharging. However: (1) the alarm must be recorded in the ORB, (2) the OWS must not be bypassed to continue discharge, (3) the cause must be investigated — filter change, coalescer cleaning, or incoming oil concentration too high. Resetting the alarm without investigation and continuing is the start of the violation path.
Common OWS operational issues: Emulsified oil (detergents in bilge prevent separation — do not use detergents to clean the bilge), excessive dirt/sediment clogging filters (bilge water quality management reduces this), high oil content in incoming bilge from a fuel leak or spill (identify and fix the source before running OWS), and temperature effects on separation efficiency (cold bilge water separates more slowly).

Module 4 · Operational Decision-Making
Legal vs Illegal — The Exact Line Between Compliance and Crime
These are not grey areas — classify each scenario as Legal, Borderline, or Criminal and understand why
▶ READ — The decision framework
✅
Legal OWS dischargeAll four conditions simultaneously met: ship proceeding en route (moving), position outside Special Areas, OWS running with 15ppm alarm operative, and effluent confirmed below 15ppm. ORB entry made contemporaneously. This is the only legally permissible overboard path for machinery space bilge water.
⚠️
Borderline — high risk, potential violationLegal on paper but operationally dangerous. Examples: discharging in heavy weather that may affect OWS separation efficiency; discharging with a bilge water containing cleaning agents that may affect the 15ppm monitor reading; approaching a Special Area with OWS still running; discharging with an OWS alarm that "sometimes triggers." These require caution and documentation.
🔴
Criminal — zero defence positionAny discharge bypassing the OWS; discharge in a Special Area; discharge while at anchor or manoeuvring; using a "magic pipe" or hose overboard; resetting the 15ppm alarm to continue discharge; making false ORB entries; instructing crew to falsify records; pumping overboard directly from the bilge well. All criminal. All prosecuted. All have resulted in convictions.

Module 5 · Real Convictions · USCG Investigation Methods
Prohibited Actions, Magic Pipe, and Real Convictions
This module uses real cases. These are not hypothetical — these are people who went to prison for bilge violations
▶ READ — Prohibited actions
🔴
"Magic pipe" — the most common methodA flexible hose, temporary pipe, or pump connection that bypasses the OWS and pumps bilge water directly overboard. Often rigged and removed quickly. Leaves no ORB trace — which itself is evidence. Detected by: physical inspection finding connection points, crew witness testimony, oily discharge sightings, suspicious ORB gaps, forensic analysis of seawater around the vessel at port entry. This is not sophisticated — it is caught.
📊
False ORB entriesFabricating or back-filling ORB entries to show OWS operations that did not occur, or to match a volume number that doesn't correspond to actual OWS run time. Detected by: OWS hour meter logs (cannot be falsified on modern units), bilge alarm records in OWS memory (each alarm is timestamped), bilge holding tank sounding records, and crew testimony. A false entry in one place creates an implausible number somewhere else — investigators are trained to find these.
🔑
Bypassing the 15ppm alarmPhysically defeating or bridging the 15ppm alarm/automatic valve so OWS continues to discharge when oil content exceeds 15ppm. Detected by: physical inspection of alarm wiring, finding bridging connections, alarm records showing no activation despite periods when the OWS could not realistically have maintained below 15ppm.
🤐
Crew whistleblowers — the most effective detection methodThe vast majority of bilge violation prosecutions originate from crew members reporting violations to the USCG or other authorities — often before the vessel arrives in port. Crew can report anonymously. The USA actively encourages reporting with financial rewards. Junior officers and ratings have reported senior officers. There is no "crew solidarity" protection when this involves criminal activity.
▶ Real conviction case studies
⛓️ Conviction Case 1 — USA
Princess Cruise Lines — M/S Regal Princess and others
Princess Cruises pleaded guilty to seven felony counts related to illegal discharge and falsification of Oil Record Books across multiple vessels. The investigation began after a crew member reported the violations to the USCG before the vessel entered a US port. The ORB showed OWS discharges that could not have occurred given the OWS hour meter readings.
💰 Fine: $40 million USD — largest MARPOL criminal fine at the time. 5-year court-supervised environmental compliance program. Individual Chief Engineers and Officers prosecuted separately.
⚠ Lesson: The whistleblower received a financial reward. The ORB hour meter discrepancy was the forensic key. The cover-up was worse than the original violation.
⛓️ Conviction Case 2 — International
Chief Engineer — Willful Violation and Cover-Up
A Chief Engineer on a bulk carrier ordered junior engineers to pump bilge water overboard using a bypass hose while in the Indian Ocean. ORB entries were made showing OWS operation for the same period. When the vessel called at a US port, the USCG inspected the OWS hour meter — it showed 8 hours of operation less than the ORB claimed. A junior engineer testified to witnessing the bypass. The Chief Engineer and the shipping company were prosecuted.
⛓️ Chief Engineer: 18 months imprisonment + $10,000 personal fine. Company: $2.5 million fine. Vessel: Port State Control detention until full audit completed.
⚠ Lesson: The OWS hour meter is an independent witness. The junior engineer who testified had initially participated but was offered immunity in exchange for cooperation. Participation in the cover-up made all participants liable.
⛓️ Conviction Case 3 — USCG Investigation Method
The Fluorescence Test — Port Entry Detection
When a vessel arrives at a US port, USCG inspectors may collect seawater samples from around the hull. These are tested for petroleum fluorescence. If the seawater shows petroleum traces consistent with bilge discharge, this creates probable cause for a detailed ORB investigation. This test has triggered prosecutions even when the vessel's ORB appeared compliant — the chemistry doesn't lie.
Multiple convictions have resulted from seawater sampling combined with OWS memory data and crew interviews.
⚠ Lesson: Even a "careful" bypass that avoids ORB gaps can be detected at port entry by seawater chemistry. There is no safe method of illegal discharge — only undetected ones waiting to be discovered.

Module 6 · MARPOL Annex I Reg.12–13 · Port Reception
Bilge Holding Tank Management and Port Reception Facilities
Legal alternatives to OWS discharge — managing the bilge holding tank, slop transfers, and port reception facility obligations
▶ READ
🗃️
Bilge holding tank — the primary bufferThe bilge holding tank (or dirty oil tank) holds accumulated bilge water until it can be processed by the OWS or discharged to a port reception facility. It must have capacity sufficient for the vessel's trading pattern — approaching port with a holding tank near capacity and no OWS capacity is a predictable management failure, not a force majeure event.
📊
Sounding records — the bilge audit trailBilge holding tank soundings must be taken and recorded regularly. These records are cross-checked against ORB entries by investigators. If the holding tank level does not decrease during periods when OWS discharge is claimed in the ORB, or decreases without any recorded transfer, the discrepancy is investigated. Maintain a separate bilge sounding log updated daily.
🏭
Port reception facilities — legal discharge ashoreMARPOL requires port states to provide adequate reception facilities. Every major port has them. The vessel notifies the port at least 24 hours before arrival (Regulation 12) of the estimated quantity of bilge water for discharge. On arrival, the bilge is pumped to the facility's reception tank. The facility issues a receipt — this receipt is retained as part of the ORB documentation.
🛢️
Slop tank transferOily bilge water may be transferred to the slop tank where it is stored and combined with other oily residues. This must be recorded in the ORB (Code E). The slop tank sounding before and after transfer must be recorded. This is a legitimate management option — particularly in Special Areas where OWS discharge is prohibited. It does not dispose of the problem but defers it to a compliant port discharge.
Good bilge management starts with reducing bilge generation — not managing a growing pile. Keep machinery space clean and dry. Fix leaks promptly. Do not use detergents in the bilge (they emulsify oil and make OWS treatment impossible). Seal drip trays. A well-maintained engine room generates far less bilge water than a poorly maintained one — and the OWS can handle what it generates.
