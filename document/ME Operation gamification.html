Module 1: Main Engine Types and Operation Principles

Understanding the differences between two-stroke slow-speed and four-stroke medium-speed diesel engines is fundamental to effective watchkeeping and maintenance.

Two-Stroke Slow-Speed Engines

Two-stroke engines complete one power cycle in two strokes of the piston. They are commonly used in large cargo vessels due to their high efficiency and reliability.

  • One power stroke per revolution
  • Lower fuel consumption (SFOC: 170-180 g/kWh)
  • Simpler design with fewer moving parts
  • Scavenging air blows out exhaust gases
  • Scavenge pressure monitoring critical for performance
  • Operating speed: 50-120 RPM

Four-Stroke Medium-Speed Engines

Four-stroke engines complete one power cycle in four strokes. They are used in smaller vessels and auxiliary power generation.

  • One power stroke per two revolutions
  • Higher fuel consumption (SFOC: 200-220 g/kWh)
  • More complex valve train
  • Separate scavenging and exhaust strokes
  • Operating speed: 300-1200 RPM

Module 2: Daily Watchkeeping and Parameter Reading

Effective watchkeeping requires constant monitoring of engine parameters to detect abnormalities early and prevent damage.

Key Parameters to Monitor

  • Exhaust Temperature: 350-380°C (normal range)
  • Scavenge Pressure: 1.8-2.2 bar
  • Bearing Temperature: 60-80°C
  • Fuel Pressure: 200-250 bar
  • Engine RPM: Should remain constant

Module 3: Engine Performance Monitoring and Trends

Monitoring performance trends over days and weeks helps identify developing problems before they become critical.

Performance Indicators

  • SFOC (Specific Fuel Oil Consumption): Should remain stable; increasing SFOC indicates deteriorating condition
  • Exhaust Temperature Trends: Large differences between cylinders indicate fuel injection problems
  • Turbocharger Performance: Boost pressure should remain stable
  • Bearing Temperature Trends: Rising temperatures indicate lubrication issues

Module 5: Common Fault Conditions and Emergency Procedures

Recognizing fault conditions and taking appropriate action is critical for engine protection and crew safety.

Common Fault Conditions and Responses

  • High Exhaust Temperature (410°C+): Reduce load, check fuel injection timing and nozzles
  • High Bearing Temperature (95°C+): Check lubrication flow, reduce speed, prepare for shutdown
  • Severe Vibration: Reduce speed, check bearings and crankshaft, prepare for emergency shutdown
  • Oil Pressure Drop (Below 1 bar): Investigate immediately, prepare for emergency shutdown
