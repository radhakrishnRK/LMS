Threat Picture and Watchkeeping

Understand piracy, armed robbery, suspicious approaches, threat levels and why early detection matters.

Narration

Piracy and armed robbery prevention begins before the event. A vigilant watch, clear reporting, and an alert bridge team provide the earliest warning. Do not wait for positive identification of weapons before escalating a suspicious approach.

Course briefing

Piracy normally refers to attacks on the high seas; armed robbery generally refers to attacks in territorial waters or ports. For crew action, the priority is the same: detect early, report clearly, protect the crew, and follow the ship security plan.

Suspicious craft may approach from stern quarters, shadow the vessel, alter course with the ship, or attempt to close at high speed. Small craft are not automatically hostile, but suspicious behaviour must be challenged and reported.

Bridge, engine room, deck and security watches must be integrated. One person sees the contact; the entire ship responds through the agreed procedure.

Hardening and Deterrence Measures

Apply BMP-style layers: barriers, lighting, access control, citadel readiness and crew protection.

Narration

Ship hardening does not make the vessel invincible. It buys time. Time allows the ship to manoeuvre, raise alarms, contact authorities, and protect the crew. Every barrier must be planned before entering the risk area.

Course briefing

Physical hardening may include razor wire where permitted, secured external doors, controlled access points, protected bridge wings, locked stores, fire hoses rigged and deck lighting managed according to the security plan.

The citadel or safe muster area is only effective when communications, ventilation, water, emergency power, and the accountability system have been planned and tested.

Do not create barriers that trap crew outside or prevent emergency escape. Hardening must be coordinated with fire, evacuation and rescue arrangements.

Reporting and Communication

Use UKMTO, IMB PRC, company procedures and clear internal reporting.

Narration

Reporting is not paperwork. It is a safety control. Early, accurate reporting helps authorities build the maritime picture and can bring assistance sooner. Internally, every message must be short, factual and repeated if needed.

Course briefing

Reports should include vessel name, position, course, speed, freeboard, nature of suspicious activity, number and type of craft, weapons if seen, direction of approach, and actions taken.

UKMTO is commonly used for voluntary reporting in the Western Indian Ocean and surrounding risk areas; IMB Piracy Reporting Centre is a global 24-hour point for reporting piracy and armed robbery incidents. Company procedures may require parallel reporting to CSO/DPAs.

Crew should avoid speculation. Report what is observed: bearing, range, speed, visual description, number of persons, ladders, weapons, hooks, skiffs, mothership, or boarding attempt.

Attack Response and Crew Muster

Take immediate coordinated action: alarm, manoeuvre, increase speed, muster, protect crew and avoid confrontation.

Narration

When an attack develops, confusion is the enemy. Every person must know where to go and what to do. The bridge commands the ship response, the crew protects themselves, and no one becomes a hero on deck.

Course briefing

Immediate actions may include raising the alarm, informing the Master and CSO, increasing speed if safe, manoeuvring, activating SSAS if appropriate, using lights and sound signals, rigging water spray if part of plan, and mustering crew away from exposed areas.

Crew should not confront attackers, throw objects, display weapons, or remain on exposed deck areas. The aim is to keep crew alive, maintain control of the vessel, and buy time.

If boarding occurs, follow the company and ship security plan. Crew accountability is critical: everyone must be accounted for before any citadel or safe area is secured.

Anchorage and Port Precautions

Prevent armed robbery through access control, lighting, patrols and valuables control.

Narration

Many armed robberies happen when ships are slow, stopped or at anchor. The threat may be theft, but the risk to crew is real. The safest response is controlled access, visible alertness and immediate reporting.

Course briefing

At anchor, maintain a proper deck and security watch as required by the Ship Security Plan. Use lighting, locked access, patrols, anti-climb measures and clear communication between deck and bridge.

Secure stores, paint lockers, ropes, spare parts and portable equipment. Robbers often target stores and engine spares but may become violent when surprised.

Do not chase robbers into dark areas. Raise the alarm, illuminate the area, muster support, report to port/authorities and record facts.

After an Incident — Evidence, Welfare and Reporting

Preserve evidence, support crew, report accurately and restore security.

Narration

The event does not end when attackers leave. Crew welfare, evidence preservation, medical care, accurate reporting and lessons learned are all part of the response. A calm and structured follow-up prevents secondary harm.

Course briefing

After an incident, account for all crew, treat injuries, check watertight integrity, machinery status and fire risk. Do not disturb evidence unless needed for safety.

Record times, positions, descriptions, photographs if safe, damage, stolen items, communications and actions taken. Reports must go to company, flag, coastal state and reporting centres as required.

Crew may suffer stress, fear or guilt after an event. Welfare support, debriefing and rest are part of responsible shipboard leadership.
