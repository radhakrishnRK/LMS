Module 1 of 6 · Emergency organisation

What Crowd Management Really Means

Understand your role in controlling people, not merely giving instructions.

Crowd management is the ability to guide large groups of people calmly and safely during normal operations, drills and emergencies. On passenger ships, your job is to reduce confusion before it becomes panic, keep routes clear, account for people, and move them to the correct muster or embarkation area.
Read
👥 Crowd controlManaging a crowd is not shouting louder. It means clear routes, calm tone, visible authority, repeated instructions and controlled movement.
📍 Muster controlPeople must be guided to assigned muster stations, checked against lists, and kept ready for further instructions.
🧩 Human behaviourFear spreads quickly. People follow confident crew, simple instructions and visible order.
🧑‍🦽 Vulnerable personsChildren, elderly persons, persons with disabilities and language barriers require early identification and support.
REDBlocked route, panic, missing person, wrong muster.
YELLOWCongestion, confused passengers, unclear PA message.
GREENClear route, calm passengers, accountability complete.

Module 2 of 6 · Movement and route control

Managing Embarkation, Disembarkation and Movement

Control flow before bottlenecks become unsafe.

Embarkation and disembarkation are crowd-management operations even when there is no emergency. Control begins before people start moving. Crew must keep routes clear, prevent counter-flow, manage stairways and lifts, and ensure that persons needing assistance are not left behind.
Read
🚦 Flow controlPrevent two-way traffic on narrow routes. Use crew to control intersections.
🪧 Signage and voicePoint, gesture and repeat simple route instructions. Avoid long explanations.
👟 StairwaysKeep one side for movement. Stop running. Maintain spacing.
🧑‍🦯 AssistanceAssign helpers early for persons needing mobility or language support.

Module 3 of 6 · Communication under stress

Communication With Large Groups and Non-English Speakers

Use fewer words, stronger structure and visible signals.

In an emergency, communication must be short, repeated and action-focused. Passengers may not understand maritime terms or English. Use standard phrases, gestures, signs, crew positioning and demonstrations. Never overload a frightened crowd with long instructions.
Read
✅ Say what to do“Walk this way.” “Put on your lifejacket.” “Wait here.”
❌ Avoid jargonDo not say “Proceed to your assigned MES embarkation area” to passengers.
👋 DemonstrateShow the action. People copy visible behaviour faster than verbal orders.
🔁 RepeatOne instruction repeated calmly beats many instructions given once.
Communication formula: Attention → Action → Direction → Reassurance. Example: “Everyone, listen please. Walk slowly. Follow this crew member. You are safe.”
Activity
Select the best PA instruction
“All passengers are requested to proceed in accordance with the emergency muster list and shipboard contingency plan.”
“Please stay calm. Walk to the crew member in the yellow vest. Do not run. We will guide you.”
“There may be a developing situation. Further information will be provided after assessment.”
Module Quiz
Which instruction style works best for a stressed multilingual crowd?

Module 4 of 6 · Muster and lifejacket control

Muster Stations, Lifejackets and Accountability

Get people ready for the next decision: remain, transfer or abandon.

At muster, the objective is to account for people, calm them, prepare them and keep them ready. Crew must check lifejackets, keep families together where possible, identify missing persons and communicate status to the command team. Muster is not a waiting room. It is a controlled safety station.
Read
📋 HeadcountUse the muster list or passenger count system. Report missing or extra persons immediately.
🦺 LifejacketsCheck fit, ties, light and whistle. Demonstrate instead of arguing.
👨‍👩‍👧 Family groupsKeep families together unless safety requires separation. This reduces panic.
🚨 ReadinessMaintain order, silence when announcements are made, and readiness for movement.

Module 5 of 6 · Emergency embarkation

Embarkation Into Survival Craft and Transfer Control

The most dangerous movement is often the final movement.

During survival craft embarkation, crowd control becomes critical. Passengers must move only when instructed, in the correct order, without rushing or pushing. Crew must keep access controlled, manage vulnerable persons, prevent overcrowding, and confirm that embarkation instructions from the bridge or command team are understood.
Read
🛟 Embark by orderNo crowd should self-board a lifeboat or MES. Crew control the timing.
🚧 Access controlKeep embarkation area clear until survival craft is ready and command is given.
🧑‍🦽 Priority supportVulnerable persons may need additional crew and slower controlled transfer.
📡 CommunicationConfirm readiness and issues clearly to the command team.
Red condition: If passengers surge toward survival craft before it is ready, stop movement immediately and reinforce the control line with additional crew.
Activity

Module 6 of 6 · Human behaviour and leadership

Stress, Panic and Crew Leadership

People do not panic because they are weak. They panic when they lose certainty.

Leadership during crowd management is practical and visible. Your posture, voice and behaviour tell passengers whether the situation is under control. Stay calm, use short instructions, reduce uncertainty and give people a task. A passenger who knows what to do is easier to manage than a passenger left waiting without information.
Read
🧍 Visible leadershipStand where passengers can see you. Use firm but respectful authority.
🗣 Calm voiceA calm tone slows the emotional spread of panic.
🎯 Give tasks“Hold the rail.” “Stand behind the line.” “Help this child.” Tasks reduce helplessness.
🤝 RespectNever mock fear. The frightened passenger is not the enemy; confusion is.
Activity
What is the best leadership response?
Scenario:

An elderly passenger refuses to leave the cabin because he cannot find his wife.

Tell him he is delaying everyone and must move immediately.
Acknowledge his concern, assign another crew member to check/report, and escort him calmly to muster.
Leave him there and continue with the rest of the group.
