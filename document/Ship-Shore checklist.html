Module 1 of 6

Purpose, Authority and Joint Responsibility

The checklist is not paperwork. It is the signed safety agreement between the ship and the terminal before and during cargo, ballast, bunkering and tank-cleaning related operations.

What the checklist controls

Joint declarationThe responsible ship officer and terminal representative confirm that the ship/shore interface is safe.
Named responsibilityEvery item has an owner, not an assumption. If a point is not understood, cargo must not start.
Repeated verificationMany items are not one-time checks. They must be rechecked at agreed intervals during operations.

Module 2 of 6

Pre-Arrival Exchange and Terminal Interface

Good ship-shore safety starts before first line ashore. Cargo plan, rates, manifold details, safety rules, emergency shutdown and communication channels must be agreed early.

Information to exchange

Ship to terminalCargo plan, arrival condition, manifold size, pressure limits, loading/discharge sequence, segregations, slop status and special cargo hazards.
Terminal to shipBerth data, shore arms/hoses, rates, max pressure, vapour return requirements, emergency contacts, terminal safety rules and alarms.
Both sidesAgree start rate, maximum rate, topping-off rate, emergency signal, stop sequence and who has authority to stop operations.

Module 3 of 6

Access, Moorings, Smoking and Naked Lights

The checklist protects the physical interface: safe gangway, emergency escape, tending moorings, controlled access, and strict ignition control in hazardous areas.

STOPGangway unsafe, mooring lines too slack/tight, unauthorised smoking, non-Ex equipment on deck, or uncontrolled visitors.
VERIFYWeather, tide, surge, passing traffic, shore gangway load, security plan and emergency towing wires where required.
PROCEEDAccess safe, watch posted, moorings tended, fire wires/ETOPS as applicable, and no smoking/naked-light rules confirmed.

Module 4 of 6

Cargo Transfer: Rates, Pressure, ESD and Communications

Most ship-shore incidents are not caused by lack of effort. They are caused by unclear agreement, wrong line-up, poor communication, or a pressure/rate change that was not managed.

Critical transfer controls

Start slowBegin at the agreed start rate. Check lines, manifold, valves, shore arm movement and tank response.
Pressure disciplineNever exceed agreed maximum manifold pressure. Any unexpected pressure rise must be treated as a warning.
Emergency shutdownShip and shore must agree the ESD signal and test communication before transfer starts.
Communication intervalAgree routine call intervals and mandatory calls for rate changes, topping-off, stripping, stoppage and emergency.

Module 5 of 6

Monitoring During Operations and Repeat Checks

Signing the checklist does not make the operation safe. The operation remains safe only if the checklist conditions are maintained and rechecked until completion.

Watchkeeper monitoring loop

Deck roundsCheck manifolds, hoses/arms, scuppers, drip trays, ullages, tank vents, moorings and overboard discharges.
Log and compareCompare ship and shore figures. Unexplained difference means stop, investigate and reconcile.
Weather and movementWind, squall, tide, swell, surge and passing traffic may change a safe berth into an unsafe berth.
Repeat checksItems marked for repeat check must be verified at agreed intervals, not only at the safety meeting.

Module 6 of 6

Records, Close-Out, Inspections and Non-Conformities

The checklist becomes evidence. It must show that safety was verified, intervals were followed, changes were agreed, and any deviation was stopped, corrected and recorded.

STOP and recordLeak, failed communications, unsafe access, pressure breach, gas alarm, unauthorised hot work, or checklist item no longer true.
Amend and countersignAny change to plan, rate, sequence, berth condition, venting, personnel, watch arrangement or terminal requirement.
Close outComplete final figures, drain lines safely, close valves, disconnect correctly, confirm no pollution and file records.
