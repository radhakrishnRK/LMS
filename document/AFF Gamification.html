Module 1 of 6 · STCW A-VI/3

Command and Organisation of Fire Operations

Roles, responsibilities and tactical decision-making at the command level

▶ READ
🎯
Fire Commander roleTakes command from the bridge. Controls all fire-fighting resources. Maintains communication with Master. Never enters the fire zone — commands from a safe position.
⚔️
Attack teamAdvances into the fire zone with hoses and BA sets. Minimum 2 persons. Always has a charged backup line. Timed entry and exit — tracked by the Fire Commander.
🛡️
Boundary teamCools adjacent bulkheads and decks. Prevents fire spread. Checks for hot spots on the reverse side. Reports temperature changes to the Fire Commander.
🔧
Support teamOperates fixed systems on command. Manages backup equipment. Provides relief for BA wearers. Controls access to the fire zone — no unauthorised entry.
The Fire Commander never enters the fire zone. Command is useless if the commander is incapacitated. Maintain command position, communication, and situational awareness at all times.
Communication discipline: All teams report status on a dedicated fire channel. Reports use position + status format: "Attack team — Deck 4 Fr.40 — fire reduced, advancing." No informal chatter on the fire channel.

Case Study — Real Incident
MV Hanjin Pennsylvania — Engine Room Fire, 2002
A container vessel suffered a series of explosions and fire in the engine room. Two crew members died. Investigation found that the fire party entered the engine room before boundary cooling was established, and the CO₂ system was delayed. Command communications broke down early.
⚠ Lesson: Establish command, boundary cooling, and communications BEFORE committing attack teams to an enclosed space fire.

Fire-Fighting Agents — Selection, Deployment, Limitations

Choosing the right agent is a command decision — wrong choice can be fatal

▶ READ
Agent Class Action Key limitation
Water (jet) A Cools — removes heat Spreads burning oil; never on live electrical or Class B
Water spray / fog A Cools + smothers Limited penetration; requires close approach
Foam (AFFF) B Smothers — excludes O₂ Ineffective on Class C/gas; use foam branch correctly
CO₂ B/C/E Displaces oxygen Lethal in occupied spaces; no cooling; re-ignition risk
Dry powder (DCP) B/C E Chemical chain interruption Zero visibility; damages equipment; no cooling
Water mist A E Cools + smothers + absorbs heat Requires purpose-designed nozzles; less effective outdoors
CO₂ in an occupied space is fatal. Before activating CO₂, verify by positive headcount that all personnel are clear. Two independent verifications are required. No exceptions.

Module 3 of 6 · STCW A-VI/3

Fixed Fire Suppression Systems

CO₂, foam, dry powder, water mist — operation, activation, and critical limitations

▶ READ
🔴
CO₂ total floodingDesigned for unoccupied engine rooms and cargo holds. Two-step activation: pilot cylinders then main bank. Time delay built in. Once fired — irreversible. Cannot enter the space.
🟡
Fixed foam systemsTanker cargo deck protection. Low-expansion foam for surface application. Proportioner must be correctly set. Check foam concentration regularly — degraded foam fails silently.
🔵
Water mist (NOVEC/Hi-Fog)High-pressure fine droplets cool, smother and absorb radiant heat. Safe for personnel exposure unlike CO₂. Effective in machinery spaces, accommodation, galleys.
🟢
Detection and alarmConfirm zone before activation. Two detector triggers typically required to avoid spurious activation. Know your panel — identify the zone, not just the alarm.
Before activating any fixed suppression system: (1) Confirm the fire is real — not a sensor fault. (2) Confirm the space is clear of personnel. (3) Isolate ventilation to the space. (4) Confirm boundaries are in place. Then activate on the Fire Commander's order.

Case Study — Real Incident
MV Bow Mariner — Tanker Fire, 2004
A chemical tanker exploded and sank off the US East Coast. 21 crew died. The fire suppression system was not activated promptly. Investigation found the CO₂ system valves were not opened in the correct sequence, and crew had not practised the procedure.
⚠ Lesson: Every officer responsible for fixed system activation must practise the procedure physically — not just read it. Drills must include actual valve operation, not only theory.

Boundary Cooling and Smoke Management

Preventing fire spread is often more important than attacking the fire itself

▶ READ
🌡️
Why boundary cooling mattersSteel conducts heat rapidly. A bulkhead adjacent to a fire can reach 200°C+ on the reverse side. Above 60°C on a boundary — cool immediately. Above 80°C — the space beyond may already be on fire.
💧
Cooling techniqueStart cooling from the top of the boundary (heat rises). Apply water in sweeping motions — do not hold the jet in one spot. Check all six sides of a compartment: four bulkheads, deck, and deckhead.
💨
Smoke managementSmoke is toxic, flammable and reduces visibility to zero. Secure ventilation to starve the fire and prevent smoke spread. Do not open doors to smoke-filled spaces without BA and backup line in place.
🔀
Ventilation controlClose all ventilation dampers serving the fire zone. Do not use mechanical ventilation to clear smoke until the fire is confirmed out — this fans the fire and spreads smoke. Natural ventilation (opening hatch to weather) only when safe.
Never open a boundary door to a smoke-filled space without: (1) BA set on and positive pressure checked, (2) charged hose in hand, (3) backup team in position, (4) Fire Commander's permission. An unopened door is a fire stop. An opened door is an oxygen supply.

Module 5 of 6 · STCW A-VI/3

Casualties in Fire Situations and Post-Fire Actions

Managing casualties without compromising the fire operation — and securing after the fire is out

▶ READ
🏥
Smoke inhalation — primary killerCarbon monoxide binds to haemoglobin 200× more effectively than oxygen. Casualty may appear fine but be critically poisoned. All smoke casualties need 100% O₂ and medical assessment — no exceptions.
🫁
BA casualty rescueNever send a single rescuer. A rescue team of minimum 2 with full BA and charged line. The rescue team does not fight the fire — their only task is the casualty. Attack team continues.
🔥
Burns at seaCool burns with cool water for minimum 10 minutes — no ice. Cover with non-fluffy dressing. Do not remove clothing stuck to burns. For chemical burns — flush copiously, decontaminate, treat for systemic absorption.
✅
Post-fire — fire watchAfter the fire is declared out: establish a 2-hour minimum fire watch with thermometer checks every 15 minutes. Do not restore ventilation until boundaries confirm cool. Document all damage before any cleaning begins.
Post-fire re-ignition is common. Smouldering materials can reignite hours later, particularly in insulation, foam furniture and electrical equipment. A declared-out fire requires continuous monitoring — not a single check.
Preserving evidence: Do not disturb the fire scene beyond what is necessary for safety. Photograph everything before cleaning. Preserve any suspected ignition sources. The flag state and P&I club will require an investigation.

Case Study — Real Incident
MV Sincerity Ace — Car Carrier Fire, 2018
A PCTC caught fire mid-Pacific with 22 crew aboard. The fire party could not effectively fight the fire as vehicles were densely packed and hose access was blocked. Five crew died attempting to fight the fire. The vessel was eventually lost.
⚠ Lesson: When fighting conditions are impossible, the decision to protect boundaries and withdraw is a command decision — not a failure. Saving crew lives takes priority. Abandonment must be prepared as a parallel action.

Post-Fire Investigation and SMS Integration

Learning from fire incidents — connecting operations to the safety management system

▶ READ
🔍
Fire investigation at command levelEstablish cause of ignition, fuel source, and why the fire spread. Identify detection, suppression, and response failures. Document V-pattern burn marks — fire spreads upward and outward from ignition point.
📋
SMS obligations post-fireComplete a non-conformity report. Issue a near-miss/incident report to the company within 24 hours. Preserve all records: fire log, system activation records, radio logs, deck log entries.
🔧
System review after fireAll fire-fighting equipment used must be replaced/recharged before departure. Test all suppression systems that activated. Survey detection systems — check for fire damage to sensors.
📚
Refresher training — 5-year cycleSTCW requires AFF refresher every 5 years. Refresher must include practical fire-fighting. Theory alone does not satisfy the requirement. Keep training records in the SMS matrix.
Lessons from real fires become your training. Every significant maritime fire generates a Marine Casualty Investigation report. Reading these reports — from the MAIB, NTSB, and flag states — is one of the best forms of fire command continuing education available.
