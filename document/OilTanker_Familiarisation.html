Tanker Types and Characteristics
Understanding what kind of vessel you are on — and why it matters for safety
▶ READ
Tanker type Cargo Key safety feature
VLCC / Suezmax Crude oil — single grade, very large volumes Inert gas system mandatory. Large confined space risk in cargo tanks.
Product tanker Refined products — multiple grades, smaller parcels Multiple pump rooms, coated tanks. Grade segregation critical.
Bitumen/asphalt tanker Hot cargo — up to 200°C Thermal burns risk. Heating coils. Vapour management essential.
OBO (Oil-Bulk-Ore) Oil voyages alternating with dry bulk Residue removal critical before bulk cargo. Gas-free certification.
Shuttle tanker Crude oil from offshore production Dynamic positioning + tandem mooring. No shore berth operations.
🏗️
Key tanker structuresCargo tanks (below main deck), pump room (below main deck — highest enclosed space risk), inert gas plant, manifold (port and starboard connections to shore), bow thruster room, void spaces and cofferdams.
📍
Why tanker type matters for safetyThe cargo determines the hazards. Crude oil carries different vapour risks to aviation fuel. The ship's structure determines where danger zones are. Your familiarisation is type-specific — what applies on one tanker may not apply on another.

Cargo Properties — Flammability, Toxicity, Vapour
The cargo you carry wants to burn or poison you. Know its properties before you go anywhere near it.
▶ READ
🔥
Flammability — the dangerous rangeEvery petroleum product has a Flammable Range between the Lower Explosive Limit (LEL) and Upper Explosive Limit (UEL). Inside this range — any ignition source causes an explosion. The goal is always to keep the atmosphere either below LEL or above UEL using inert gas.
💨
Vapour density — it sinksPetroleum vapour is heavier than air (density >1). It collects in the lowest points of a vessel: pump rooms, bilges, below-deck voids, tank tops. This is why pump rooms are the most dangerous spaces on a tanker even when no cargo loading is happening.
☠️
Hydrogen sulphide (H₂S)Present in crude oil and some products. Smells of rotten eggs at low concentrations — but at dangerous levels your sense of smell is overwhelmed instantly. You smell nothing, and you die. Always use a fixed or portable gas detector — never trust your nose alone.
📊
Flash point — know your cargoFlash point is the minimum temperature at which a liquid gives off enough vapour to ignite. Crude oil: below 60°C (often −30°C). Aviation fuel: below −43°C. Diesel: above 60°C. The lower the flash point, the higher the immediate risk.
Never trust your nose on a tanker. H₂S paralyses your sense of smell at concentrations that are immediately lethal. A tank or pump room that smells "fine" can contain gas that kills you before you can react. Always test with instruments first.

Ignition Source Control and Tanker Safety Rules
Every ignition source on a tanker is a potential trigger for a catastrophic explosion
▶ READ
The tanker golden rule: No naked flames, no smoking except in the designated area, and no non-intrinsically safe electrical equipment anywhere near a cargo area, pump room, or open tank. One spark in a flammable atmosphere kills everyone on board.
🚬
Smoking policySmoking is only permitted in the designated smoking room with a self-closing metal door and an ashtray. Smoking is never permitted on deck, in cargo areas, or in the accommodation unless in the smoking room. E-cigarettes are treated the same as regular cigarettes.
⚡
Electrical equipmentOnly intrinsically safe (IS) or explosion-proof (Ex) equipment may be used in hazardous areas. Portable radios, mobile phones, cameras, and ordinary torches are all forbidden on deck during cargo operations or at anchor with open tanks.
🔨
Hot workWelding, grinding, cutting — any work that produces heat or sparks — is "hot work". A hot work permit signed by the Master is required. The area must be confirmed gas-free before and continuously during hot work. No hot work near open tanks or cargo areas.
⚙️
Static electricityCargo flow through pipes generates static electricity. Correct bonding connections between ship and shore must be made before cargo transfer. Never use non-conductive hoses or improper bonding — this is a common cause of tanker fires.
📡
Inert gas system (IGS)The IGS replaces the air in cargo tanks with inert gas (usually exhaust gas or nitrogen) that will not support combustion. When tank atmosphere is above UEL — it cannot ignite. IGS must be running during cargo discharge and tank washing. Tank O₂ content must be maintained below 8%.
🌩️
Electrical stormsStop cargo operations if lightning is sighted within 5 nautical miles. Close all cargo tank openings. Wait until the storm passes before resuming. Static from lightning is an ignition risk even without a direct strike.

PPE and Safe Access to Tanker Spaces
Enclosed spaces kill more tanker crew than cargo fires — know your PPE and the permit system
▶ READ
🦺
Minimum PPE on deck during operationsBoilersuit (coveralls), safety helmet, safety footwear, safety glasses. Chemical-resistant gloves and goggles when handling cargo samples or chemicals. No loose clothing, no open-toe footwear — ever.
😷
Respiratory protectionAir-line breathing apparatus (ELSA/SCBA) for tank entry and high H₂S areas. Escape sets (EEBD) for emergency escape only — not entry. Air-purifying respirators (APR/half-masks) only if the atmosphere is confirmed oxygen-adequate and below IDLH levels.
🔒
Enclosed space entry — permit requiredA permit to work in enclosed spaces is mandatory for every entry into cargo tanks, pump rooms, void spaces, cofferdams, and any enclosed space on a tanker. The permit requires: atmosphere testing, standby person, rescue equipment ready, communication established.
📟
Portable gas detector — alwaysEvery person entering or working near a tanker cargo area must carry or have access to a portable multi-gas detector measuring: O₂ (normal: 20.8%), LEL/% gas, H₂S (ppm), CO (ppm). Below 19.5% O₂ — do not enter. Above 1% LEL — do not enter.
The pump room is the most dangerous space on the ship. It has poor ventilation, contains cargo vapour from piping systems, and H₂S can accumulate without warning. Never enter the pump room without a full atmosphere test and a standby person maintaining two-way communication throughout.
Never enter a tanker enclosed space alone. You need: a permit, a standby person at the entrance, tested atmosphere, SCBA ready to deploy, and a rescue plan in place. If any of these is missing — do not enter.

Ship-Shore Safety Checklist and Your Role
The Ship-Shore Safety Checklist is not paperwork — it is the agreed safety state for cargo transfer
▶ READ
📋
What is the Ship-Shore Safety Checklist?A mandatory checklist completed jointly by the ship's officer and the terminal representative before cargo transfer begins. It verifies that both sides have agreed on: communication, emergency procedures, cargo limits, mooring, fire-fighting readiness, and personnel notifications.
🤝
Key items YOU must knowEmergency stop procedure (who calls it and how). Agreed communication channel (VHF channel — usually channel 16 for emergency). Maximum loading/discharge rate. Maximum cargo pressure. Warning signal for leaks or emergencies.
🔴
Emergency stop (ESD)Either the ship or terminal can call an emergency stop at any time. The response is: stop all cargo pumps, close all valves, notify both sides. The watchkeeping officer must know the ESD procedure cold — without needing to look it up.
👤
Crew role during cargo operationsMaintain continuous watch. Monitor manifold for leaks. Check hose/arm connections regularly. Maintain gangway watch. Never leave manifold area unattended. Report any change in condition, smell, or cargo system pressure immediately.
The watch officer's primary job during cargo is vigilance. A 30-second response to a leak at the manifold is the difference between a spillage and a fire. Know what normal looks like so you immediately recognise abnormal.

Emergency Procedures — Cargo Fire, Spillage, Gas Release
Three emergencies specific to oil tankers. Know your immediate actions before you ever set foot on board.
▶ READ
Emergency Immediate action What NOT to do
🔥 Cargo fire at manifold Sound alarm. Stop cargo immediately (ESD). Notify terminal. Shut all valves. Apply fixed foam if available. Muster fire party. Prepare to disconnect hoses if safe. Do not use water jet on burning oil. Do not disconnect hoses until instructed. Never re-open valves once cargo stops.
🛢️ Cargo spillage on deck Stop cargo. Notify terminal. Contain the spill — close scuppers. Prevent ignition: remove all sources. Muster response team. Prepare for notification under MARPOL. Do not wash spill overboard — MARPOL violation. Do not approach without PPE. Do not ignore small spills — they spread.
☠️ Toxic gas release (H₂S) Sound alarm. Put on SCBA immediately. Do not approach without BA. Account for all personnel. Evacuate affected area. Notify terminal. TMAS consultation for any casualties. Do not enter the area without SCBA — you will be incapacitated within seconds. Do not wait to see if it clears.
MARPOL applies to every tanker worldwide. Any oil spillage to the sea must be reported. Attempting to conceal a spill is a criminal offence for the Master. Notify the terminal, flag state, and coastal state if in territorial waters.
